"The starting gun — formally authorises the project and empowers the PM"

The Project Charter is the PM's passport — it grants authority over resources and formally declares the project exists. Senior Management signs it; the PM uses it.

💡 Insight

The Charter is initiated by the Project Sponsor or Senior Management — never by the PM alone. It is created BEFORE detailed planning begins and provides only a high-level picture of budget, schedule, and requirements.

Product Scope vs. Project Scope:
  • Product Scope — Features, functions, and characteristics of the product/service/result
  • Project Scope — The work required to deliver the product scope
  • Measured against the product requirements (product scope) and PM Plan (project scope)
Scope Creep vs. Gold Plating:
  • Scope Creep — Unauthorised expansion of scope; driven by stakeholders asking for "just one more thing"
  • Gold Plating — PM/team adds extra features not in scope; may seem helpful but wastes resources and is not approved
  • Both are BAD — both must be prevented

⭐ Requirements Traceability Matrix (RTM)

Links each requirement to its source (stakeholder) and traces it through deliverables and test cases. Contains: requirement ID, source, priority, current status (active/cancelled/deferred/added/approved/completed), validation method. Used to manage scope changes and ensure nothing is missed.

Prototypes:

Creating a working model of the expected product allows stakeholders to interact and provide feedback early. Reduces rework later. Especially useful for software and product design.

Observation / Job Shadowing:

Watching users perform their work to understand unstated needs. Called "job shadowing" when the observer participates. Very effective for process improvement projects.

⭐ Rolling Wave Planning

A form of Progressive Elaboration. Work to be done in the near term is defined in detail. Work far in the future is left as planning packages (high-level placeholders). As the project progresses, far-term placeholders are decomposed into activities when details become known. Especially important in long or uncertain projects.

Activity List vs. Milestone List:
  • Activity List — all scheduled activities on the project with ID, description, and scope of work
  • Milestone — a significant point or event; has zero duration. Required (contractual) or optional (best practice)

⭐ PDM Dependency Relationships — Memorise All 4!
Finish-to-Start (FS) — MOST COMMON
A
→ A must FINISH before B can START
B

Example: Foundation must be poured before walls can be built.

Finish-to-Finish (FF)
A
→ A must FINISH before B can FINISH
B

Example: Document editing must finish before proofreading finishes.

Start-to-Start (SS)
A
→ A must START before B can START
B

Example: Foundation work must start before framing starts.

Start-to-Finish (SF) — RAREST
A
→ A must START before B can FINISH
B

Rarely used. Example: New system must start before old system can be shut down.

Dependency Types (4 Types):
  • Mandatory (Hard Logic) — Inherent in the work; can't be changed. "Concrete must cure before building continues."
  • Discretionary (Soft Logic) — Best practice or preference; CAN be changed if needed. "We prefer to paint before installing carpet."
  • External Dependencies — Outside the project team's control. "Waiting for government approval."
  • Internal Dependencies — Within the project team's control.
Leads vs. Lags:
  • Lead — Allows acceleration of the successor; negative time value. Start painting while plumbing is still 20% incomplete.
  • Lag — Delay between activities; positive wait time. "Concrete must cure for 2 days before next step."
Lead = negative lag
FS + Lead: Start B before A finishes
FS + Lag: Wait before starting B

⭐ Estimating Techniques Comparison
TechniqueHow It WorksAccuracySpeed & Cost
Analogous (Top-Down)Uses historical data from similar past projectsLowestFastest & Cheapest
ParametricStatistical relationship between variables (e.g. £/sq ft)MediumModerate

📐 PERT Formulas — MUST KNOW!
Beta Distribution (PERT): E = (O + 4M + P) / 6 Triangle Distribution: E = (O + M + P) / 3 Standard Deviation: σ = (P - O) / 6 Variance: σ² = ((P - O) / 6)² Example: O=6, M=10, P=14 PERT = (6 + 40 + 14) / 6 = 60/6 = 10 days σ = (14 - 6) / 6 = 8/6 = 1.33 days 68% probability: 10 ± 1.33 (8.67 to 11.33) 95% probability: 10 ± 2.67 (7.33 to 12.67) 99% probability: 10 ± 4.00 (6.00 to 14.00)
Exam Bullets — Estimate Durations
  • O = Optimistic (best case), M = Most Likely (most realistic), P = Pessimistic (worst case)
  • The Beta (PERT) formula weights Most Likely by 4 — it's the most accurate of the three-point methods
  • Analogous is most appropriate when little detail is available; gives ROM estimate
  • Parametric uses measurable units (cost per tonne, hours per function point)
  • Reserve Analysis adds contingency buffers — both time (schedule reserve) and cost reserves

⭐ Critical Path Method (CPM) — MUST KNOW!

The Critical Path is the longest sequence of activities through the network. It determines the minimum project duration. Activities on the critical path have zero float (slack).

Forward Pass (ES/EF): EF = ES + Duration - 1 Backward Pass (LS/LF): LS = LF - Duration + 1 Float (Total): Float = LS - ES = LF - EF Critical Path: All activities with Float = 0
TermMeaningFormula
ESEarly Start — earliest an activity can beginForward pass
EFEarly Finish — earliest it can endES + Duration
LSLate Start — latest it can begin without delaying projectBackward pass
LFLate Finish — latest it can end without delaying projectBackward pass
Total FloatDelay allowed without delaying project endLS − ES or LF − EF
Free FloatDelay allowed without delaying next activityES(successor) − EF
⭐ Schedule Compression — Two Methods
MethodHowCost ImpactRisk Impact
CrashingAdd more resources to critical path activities; overtime, extra staffALWAYS increases costMay increase risk
Fast TrackingPerform activities in parallel that were originally sequentialMay NOT add costINCREASES risk of rework

Key: Crashing is done on critical path only. Fast tracking creates rework risk because dependent activities run simultaneously.

Exam Bullets — Develop Schedule
  • Critical Path = zero float — any delay on the critical path delays the whole project
  • A project can have more than one critical path — increases overall risk
  • Crashing ALWAYS adds cost; Fast Tracking ALWAYS adds risk of rework
  • Resource Levelling = shifting activities to smooth resource usage — often extends the schedule
  • Monte Carlo simulates thousands of schedule scenarios to produce probability distributions
  • Schedule Baseline = the approved schedule model used to compare actual vs. planned

Cost Types — Know the Differences:
  • Fixed Costs — Remain constant regardless of output (equipment rental)
  • Variable Costs — Change with level of output (hourly labor, materials)
  • Direct Costs — Attributed directly to the project (project team salaries)
  • Indirect Costs — Overhead shared across projects (office utilities, admin)
  • Sunk Costs — Already spent; irrelevant to future decisions ("spilt milk")
Value Engineering & Life Cycle Cost:
  • Value Engineering — Finding a less costly way to do the same scope. Function over cost.
  • Life Cycle Cost — Total cost of ownership: acquisition + operational + disposal costs. Always consider full life cycle!
  • Law of Diminishing Returns — Adding resources eventually produces decreasing incremental output

📐 EMV Formula — MUST KNOW!
EMV = Probability (%) × Impact ($) Example: Risk A: 30% chance of costing $200,000 EMV(A) = 0.30 × $200,000 = $60,000 Risk B: 20% chance of costing $500,000 EMV(B) = 0.20 × $500,000 = $100,000 Total EMV for contingency = $60,000 + $100,000 = $160,000 For Opportunities: Opportunity: 40% chance of saving $150,000 EMV = 0.40 × $150,000 = $60,000 (positive value)
Monte Carlo Simulation:

Runs thousands of simulations using probability distributions for cost and schedule. Produces a range of possible outcomes with probability percentages. Answers: "What is the probability we finish by Date X?" or "What is the 90th percentile cost?"

Tornado Chart (Sensitivity Analysis):

Bar chart showing which risks have the greatest impact on the project. Longest bars at the top = highest priority risks. Shows how each variable affects the outcome when others are held constant.

EVM is the most powerful project performance measurement system ever devised — it integrates scope, schedule, and cost into a single snapshot of project health. Master EVM and you master a huge chunk of the exam.

📐 Complete EVM Formula Reference — MEMORISE ALL!
━━ THREE BASE MEASURES ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ PV = Planned Value (What was budgeted to be done by now?) EV = Earned Value (What is the budget for work actually done?) AC = Actual Cost (What did the work actually cost?) BAC = Budget at Completion (Total approved project budget) ━━ VARIANCES (negative = BAD) ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ CV = Cost Variance = EV - AC (neg = over budget) SV = Schedule Variance = EV - PV (neg = behind schedule) ━━ PERFORMANCE INDICES (>1.0 = GOOD) ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ CPI = Cost Perf. Index = EV / AC (>1 = under budget) SPI = Sched. Perf. Index = EV / PV (>1 = ahead of schedule) ━━ FORECASTING ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ EAC = Estimate at Completion = BAC / CPI (typical forecast) ETC = Estimate to Complete = EAC - AC (remaining cost) VAC = Variance at Completion = BAC - EAC (over/under at end) TCPI = To-Complete Perf. Index = (BAC-EV)/(BAC-AC) (efficiency needed) ━━ MEMORY TRICK ━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━━ CV & CPI involve AC (Actual Cost) — both about COST SV & SPI involve PV (Planned Value) — both about SCHEDULE
⭐ EVM Worked Example
Project to build 100 units in 10 weeks. BAC = $100,000 After Week 5: PV = $50,000 (should have spent $50K by week 5) EV = $40,000 (only did $40K worth of work) AC = $48,000 (actually spent $48K) CV = EV - AC = $40K - $48K = -$8,000 → OVER BUDGET SV = EV - PV = $40K - $50K = -$10,000 → BEHIND SCHEDULE CPI = EV / AC = 40/48 = 0.83 → Getting $0.83 of value per $1 spent SPI = EV / PV = 40/50 = 0.80 → Only 80% as fast as planned EAC = BAC / CPI = $100,000 / 0.83 = $120,482 (will cost MORE) ETC = EAC - AC = $120,482 - $48,000 = $72,482 (still needed) VAC = BAC - EAC = $100,000 - $120,482 = -$20,482 (over budget)

Exam Bullets — Control Costs / EVM
  • EV is always the middle value — it anchors both CV and SV calculations
  • Negative CV or SV = BAD. Positive = GOOD. Index below 1.0 = BAD. Above 1.0 = GOOD.
  • TCPI > 1.0 = need to be MORE efficient than current. TCPI < 1.0 = can afford to be less efficient.
  • EAC = BAC/CPI is the most common formula — assumes future performance mirrors past
  • If EAC is recalculated using remaining work at original rate: EAC = AC + (BAC - EV)

⭐ Control Chart Rules — Most Tested!
  • UCL = Mean + 3σ (Upper Control Limit) — NOT a specification limit
  • LCL = Mean − 3σ (Lower Control Limit)
  • Rule of Seven: 7 consecutive points on same side of mean = out of control
  • Rule of Seven: 7 consecutive points trending up OR down = out of control
  • Point outside UCL or LCL = ALWAYS out of control

EEF vs. OPA — Critical Distinction
▼
Enterprise Environmental Factors (EEF):

Things you CANNOT control — they influence the project from outside

  • Organisational culture and structure
  • Government or industry standards
  • Market conditions
  • Existing human resources (available skills)
  • Political climate
  • Project Management Information Systems (PMIS)
  • Infrastructure (facilities, equipment)
  • Stakeholder risk tolerances
Organisational Process Assets (OPA):

Things your organisation CAN control and provide to help

  • Processes, procedures, and policies
  • Templates (charter, WBS, risk register)
  • Historical information from past projects
  • Lessons learned repository
  • Financial controls and reporting formats
  • Change control procedures
  • Risk register templates
  • Quality checklists
⚠️ Exam Tip

EEF = external constraints; OPA = organisational tools and knowledge. EEF influences how you plan but you can't change them. OPA provides assets you actively use. Both are inputs to almost every process.
