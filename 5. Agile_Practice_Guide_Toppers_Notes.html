Introduction — Why This Guide, What It Covers

Purpose & Context

Jointly developed by PMI and Agile Alliance®. Bridges predictive and agile project management. Practical guidance for project leaders and team members adapting to agile. Not software-only — covers manufacturing, education, healthcare.

📌 Core Purpose
The guide provides tools, situational guidelines, and understanding of agile techniques. It addresses the "messy middle ground" between purely predictive and purely agile approaches.

Why Now?

  • Exponential advances in technology
  • Customer demand for immediate value delivery
  • Social media enabling rapid feedback loops
  • Disruptive technologies changing competitive landscape
  • Cloud computing enabling iterative/incremental releases

An Introduction to Agile — Manifesto, Mindset, Lean & Kanban

2.1 Definable Work vs. High-Uncertainty Work

Definable Work

  • Clear, proven procedures
  • Similar projects done before
  • Low execution uncertainty
  • Examples: car production, construction (post-design)
  • → Use Predictive approach

High-Uncertainty Work

  • Exploratory, not-done-before work
  • Requires collaboration & problem-solving
  • High change, complexity, and risk
  • Examples: software, product design, R&D
  • → Use Agile approach

2.2 The Agile Manifesto (2001)

Formalized by thought leaders in software industry in 2001. Now applied across many industries.

Individuals & Interactions
OVER processes and tools
People and their collaboration are more valuable than rigid processes or tools.
Working Software
OVER comprehensive documentation
Functional product is the primary measure of progress, not documents.
Customer Collaboration
OVER contract negotiation
Working with customers continuously is better than fixing requirements upfront.
Responding to Change
OVER following a plan
Adapting to change delivers better competitive advantage than rigid adherence to a plan.
⚠️ Critical Exam Point
"While there is value in the items on the right, we value the items on the LEFT more." — Items on the right (processes, documentation, contracts, plans) still have value. Agile does NOT eliminate them.

The 12 Principles of Agile

1
Satisfy customer through early and continuous delivery of valuable software — highest priority
2
Welcome changing requirements, even late. Agile harnesses change for competitive advantage.
3
Deliver working software frequently (weeks to months), prefer shorter timescale
4
Business + developers work together daily throughout the project
5
Build around motivated individuals. Give environment/support. Trust them.
6
Most effective communication = face-to-face conversation
7
Working software = primary measure of progress
8
Promote sustainable development — constant pace indefinitely
9
Continuous attention to technical excellence and good design enhances agility
10
Simplicity — art of maximizing work NOT done — essential
11
Best architectures/designs emerge from self-organizing teams
12
At regular intervals, team reflects and adjusts → Retrospective

Agile Mindset Model (Ahmed Sidky)

Agile = Mindset defined by 4 Values → guided by 12 Principles → manifested through many Practices
🔑 Key Concept
Agile approaches existed BEFORE the Manifesto (years/decades). The Manifesto formalized existing practices. Agile is a BLANKET TERM covering: Scrum, XP, Kanban, FDD, DSDM, Crystal, AUP, Scrumban — all subsets of Lean thinking.

2.3 Lean and the Kanban Method

Hierarchy
Lean (superset) → Agile + Kanban Method (named instances of lean thinking)

Shared heritage: delivering value, respecting people, minimizing waste, transparency, adapting to change, continuous improvement.

Kanban Method: emerged mid-2000s as alternative to agile methods. The original "start-where-you-are" approach. Less prescriptive, less disruptive to implement.

💡 Kanban vs Agile Debate
Kanban was conceived in lean manufacturing but widely used in agile settings. Both camps claim it. The key: Kanban optimizes flow; Agile embraces change with iterations.
Two Strategies to Fulfill Agile Values
1. Adopt a formal agile framework (learn it fully before tailoring)
2. Implement changes incrementally that fit project context

2.4 Uncertainty, Risk & Life Cycle Selection (Stacey Complexity Model)

Two axes: Requirements Uncertainty (Y) × Technical Uncertainty (X)

Simple/Complicated

Low requirements + Low technical uncertainty

→ Linear/Predictive works well

Complex

High requirements OR technical uncertainty

→ Adaptive/Agile approaches work well

Chaotic

Both uncertainties VERY HIGH

→ Need to contain one variable first

Agile/iterative/incremental approaches work well for projects with: R&D, high rates of change, unclear/unknown requirements, hard-to-describe final goals.

Iterative + Incremental = Agile Feedback Loop
Short feedback loops → Reprioritization → Frequent adaptation → Updated plans → Frequent delivery → Less waste/rework

Life Cycle Selection — Four Types, Hybrid Approaches & Tailoring

3.1 The Four Life Cycles — Master Table

Life Cycle Requirements Activities Delivery Goal Use When
Predictive Fixed Once for entire project Single delivery Manage cost Stable, well-known requirements; low risk
Iterative Dynamic Repeated until correct Single delivery Correctness of solution Complex, high change; optimize for learning
Incremental Dynamic Once per increment Frequent smaller deliveries Speed Customer needs partial value quickly
Agile Dynamic Repeated until correct Frequent small deliveries Customer value via frequent delivery + feedback High uncertainty; continuous feedback needed
💡 Key Insight — Planning is always present
What differentiates a life cycle is NOT whether planning is done, but HOW MUCH planning is done and WHEN. Predictive = most upfront. Agile = plan → deliver → replan → repeat.

Characteristics of Each Life Cycle

📋 Predictive Life Cycle

  • Plan drives the work; detailed upfront
  • High certainty: firm requirements, stable team, low risk
  • Activities execute serially: Analyze → Design → Build → Test → Deliver
  • Restricts changes to scope/requirements
  • No business value until END of project
  • Also called: plan-driven, waterfall, serial

🔄 Iterative Life Cycle

  • Improves product through successive prototypes
  • Each prototype → new stakeholder feedback → rework
  • Optimized for learning, not speed
  • Useful when: high complexity, frequent changes, differing stakeholder views
  • May take longer due to learning focus

📦 Incremental Life Cycle

  • Optimize for speed of delivery
  • Delivers finished, usable subsets of solution
  • Customer gets value BEFORE project complete
  • Each increment is fully done (fixtures, paint, everything)
  • MVP possible — deliver to subset of customers for feedback

⚡ Agile Life Cycle

  • Both iterative AND incremental
  • Expects requirements to change
  • Incremental delivery uncovers hidden requirements
  • Iteration-based: fixed timeboxes → complete features
  • Flow-based: pull from backlog based on capacity; WIP limits
  • Earlier return on investment (highest value work first)

Iteration-Based vs. Flow-Based Agile

AspectIteration-Based (e.g., Scrum)Flow-Based (e.g., Kanban)
Time structureFixed timeboxes (sprints)Continuous; no fixed timebox
Work planningPlan per iterationPull from backlog as capacity allows
Work controlIteration commitmentWIP (Work-In-Progress) limits
Standup focusWhat did I do? What will I do? Impediments?Walk board right→left; bottlenecks; flow?
Demos/reviewsEnd of each iterationWhen enough features accumulate
RetrospectivesEnd of each iterationTeam-determined schedule
MeasurementVelocity (story pts/iteration)Lead time, cycle time, throughput
RefinementTimeboxed sessions (mid-iteration)Just-in-time (as card is pulled)

3.1.6–3.1.11 Hybrid Life Cycles

⚠️ Exam Alert — Hybrid ≠ Agile
A hybrid approach uses COMBINATION of predictive, iterative, incremental, and/or agile elements. Calling a hybrid "agile" is misleading — it doesn't fully embody agile mindset/values/principles.
Agile Dev → Predictive Rollout
Agile development phase (uncertainty/complexity) followed by predictive rollout (defined, repeatable). Example: new high-tech product + mass training rollout. Pharma: agile trials → FDA approval (predictive end phase).
Combined Simultaneously
Some parts agile, some parts predictive in same project. Transitioning incrementally to agile. Common scenario — not fully agile nor fully predictive.
Mostly Predictive + Agile Components
Small agile element within mainly predictive project. Example: construction project with new roofing material — run agile trials for new component, predictive for rest.
Mostly Agile + Predictive Component
Largely agile with one non-negotiable predictive element. Example: external vendor component that cannot be delivered incrementally — single integration at end.
Fit-For-Purpose Hybrid
Designed based on project risks. Campus construction: incremental for building order (ROI acceleration) + predictive per individual building. Government insurance system: agile + predictive calculation team concurrently.
Transition Strategy Hybrid
Gradual shift from predictive to agile. Add iterative techniques first → then incremental. Try on low-risk, medium-uncertainty project. Then scale up. Tailored to organization's readiness.

3.2 Mixing Agile Approaches

Teams rarely limit to one approach. Common blend: Scrum + Kanban + XP

Popular Blend — Scrum + Kanban + XP
Scrum: product backlog, product owner, scrum master, sprints, reviews, retrospectives
Kanban board: visualize flow, make impediments visible, manage WIP limits
XP practices: story cards, continuous integration, refactoring, automated testing, TDD
⚠️ Caution on Tailoring
Tailoring is an ADVANCED topic. Gain experience and be successful with one approach BEFORE tailoring. Premature/haphazard tailoring minimizes effectiveness and limits benefits. "Shu-Ha-Ri" model: first obey (Shu) → then consciously deviate (Ha) → then innovate (Ri).

3.3 Project Factors That Influence Tailoring

Project FactorTailoring Recommendation
Steady vs. sporadic demandFlow-based agile with cadence (best of both worlds)
Inexperienced teamRetrospect more often; train fundamentals first
Frequent interruptions/delaysKanban boards + WIP limits to expose bottlenecks
Poor quality incrementsTest-driven development (TDD) practices
Multiple teams on one productLearn agile program management / scaling frameworks
New to agile approachTrain in mindset fundamentals; workshop on specific approach

Implementing Agile: Creating an Agile Environment

4.1 Start with an Agile Mindset

Managing with agile requires the team to adopt an agile mindset. Key questions to guide implementation:

  • How can the team act in an agile manner?
  • What can we deliver quickly for early feedback?
  • How can the team be transparent?
  • What work can be avoided (high-priority focus)?
  • How can servant-leadership benefit the team's goals?

4.2 Servant Leadership

🔑 Definition
Servant Leadership = the practice of leading through service to the team, by focusing on understanding and addressing the needs and development of team members to enable highest possible team performance.
Servant Leader Order of Priorities
1. Purpose — Define the "why"; team optimizes at project level, not person level
2. People — Create environment where everyone can succeed; each member contributes
3. Process — Results over perfect process; cross-functional team that delivers finished value often and reflects = agile
👂
Listening
Active, deep listening to team members
🤝
Serving
Service to team first, not command
🌱
Helping Grow
Nurture team members' development
🏋️
Coaching
Coach vs. control; enable not direct
🔒
Safety & Trust
Promote psychological safety
🧠
Self-Awareness
Know own strengths & biases

4.2.1 Servant Leader Responsibilities

Facilitate (not Manage)

  • Shift from "managing coordination" → "facilitating collaboration"
  • Help everyone do their best thinking
  • Encourage participation, understanding, shared responsibility
  • Expose and communicate bottlenecks between teams
  • Become impartial bridge-builders and coaches

Remove Organizational Impediments

  • Challenge lengthy processes (finance, change control, audits)
  • Help departments understand agile deliverables
  • Address bottleneck processes that delay delivery
  • Work with finance for incremental budgeting
  • Work with auditors in regulatory environments

Pave the Way for Others' Contribution

  • Enable self-management and self-organization
  • Work with facilities for team space
  • Enable team focus on one project at a time
  • Work with product owner to develop stories with team

Educate & Support

  • Educate stakeholders on agile benefits (prioritization, productivity, quality)
  • Mentor, encourage, advocate for training
  • Help with technical PM activities (e.g., quantitative risk analysis)
  • Celebrate successes; build external bridges

4.2.2 Role of Project Manager in Agile

⚠️ Role Shift — Critical for PMP
Traditional PM: Center of coordination, tracking, status reporting to organization
Agile PM: Servant leader — coaching, fostering collaboration, aligning stakeholder needs
The value of PMs is NOT their position, but their ability to make everyone else better.

In agile, PMs encourage distribution of responsibility to team members who have the knowledge to get work done. Cross-functional teams coordinate their own work and collaborate with product owner.

4.3 Team Composition

Cross-Functional Team Member

  • All skills needed to deliver working product within team
  • No external dependencies to complete work
  • Deliver potentially releasable product on regular cadence
  • Higher quality through collaboration
  • Key differentiator: can finish work independently

Product Owner

  • Guides product direction; ranks work by business value
  • Works with team DAILY; provides feedback
  • Creates and maintains backlog with team
  • Business background; deep subject matter expertise
  • Manages flow of work to team
  • Critical: without strong PO, team creates low-value features

Team Facilitator (Servant Leader)

  • Also: Scrum Master, Project Lead, Team Coach, PM
  • All agile teams need servant leadership
  • Facilitation, coaching, impediment removal
  • External coaches: experience but weak relationships
  • Internal coaches: strong relationships but limited breadth

4.3.1 Agile Team Attributes

AttributeGoal
Dedicated peopleFocus, productivity; small team <10
Cross-functionalDeliver finished value independently
Colocated or managed distributedBetter communication, knowledge sharing
Generalists + specialists (T-shaped)Flexibility + expertise
Stable work environmentDependency, agreed approach, run rate
I-shaped vs. T-shaped vs. Broken Comb
I-shaped: Deep in one area, no breadth — specialist silo
T-shaped: Deep specialization + broad support skills — IDEAL for agile
Broken Comb (Paint Drip): Multiple depth areas — superspecialist
Multitasking = Productivity Loss
Two projects at 50% each → actual ~20–40% on each. Loss increases exponentially with more tasks. 100% dedicated = fastest throughput.

4.3.3–4.3.7 Generalizing Specialists, Structures & Workspaces

Generalizing Specialists
T-shaped people needed for flow efficiency (team throughput) vs. resource efficiency (individual utilization). Single person's throughput is NOT relevant — team optimization matters.
Team Workspaces
"Caves and common" — balance open collaboration areas with quiet focus spaces. Fishbowl windows (long-lived video links) + remote pairing for distributed teams.
Overcoming Silos
Team members often report to different managers with different metrics. Managers must shift from resource efficiency → flow efficiency. Build cross-functional teams; dedicate individuals to one team.

Implementing Agile: Delivering in an Agile Environment

5.1 Charter the Project and the Team

Project Charter answers:

  • Why? — Project vision/purpose
  • Who benefits and how? — Stakeholders
  • What does done mean? — Release criteria
  • How will we work together? — Flow of work

Team Charter / Social Contract includes:

  • Team values (sustainable pace, core hours)
  • Working agreements (Definition of Ready, Definition of Done)
  • Ground rules (one speaker at a time)
  • Group norms (meeting times, respect)

5.2 Common Agile Practices

5.2.1 Retrospectives — THE MOST IMPORTANT PRACTICE

  • Allows team to learn, improve, and adapt process
  • Based on Principle #12: reflect at regular intervals
  • NOT about blame — look at qualitative + quantitative data
  • Find root causes → countermeasures → action plans
  • Limit action items to team's capacity (better fewer, completed)
  • Measure outcomes in next period to validate
  • Trigger times: after release, when stuck, >few weeks elapsed, any milestone

5.2.2 Backlog Preparation

  • Ordered list of work presented in story form
  • Don't create ALL stories upfront — just enough for first release (broad) + next iteration (detailed)
  • Product owner creates product roadmap showing anticipated sequence
  • Roadmap replanned based on what team produces
  • Progressive elaboration of backlog items

5.2.3 Backlog Refinement

  • PO works with team to prepare stories for upcoming iteration
  • Goal: stories sized and understood by team
  • Refinement spectrum: just-in-time (flow) → 1hr mid-iteration (iteration-based) → multiple sessions
  • Teams aim for ≤1 hr/week on refinement
  • Techniques: triads (dev+tester+BA), group discussion, story splitting
  • Spike if uncertainty around a feature

5.2.4 Daily Standups

  • Timebox: max 15 minutes; anyone can facilitate
  • Iteration-based: What did I complete? What will I do? Impediments?
  • Flow-based: Walk board right→left: What advances this? What must we finish? Anything not on board? Bottlenecks?
  • Antipatterns: Becomes status meeting; team solves problems during standup
  • Problems → parking lot → separate meeting after standup

5.2.5 Demonstrations / Reviews

  • Team demonstrates working product periodically
  • Product owner accepts or declines stories
  • Iteration-based: all completed work at end of iteration
  • Flow-based: when enough features accumulate
  • General rule: demo at least every 2 weeks
  • No demo = no learning = not adopting agile techniques

5.2.6 Planning for Iteration-Based Agile

  • Each team's capacity is different
  • Account for vacations/holidays → reduced capacity → reduced commitment
  • Teams estimate what they can complete (not 100% certain)
  • Agile: plan a little → deliver → learn → replan (ongoing cycle)
  • Antipattern: trying to commit to more than capacity allows

5.2.7 Execution Practices (Technical Excellence)

  • Continuous Integration: Frequent incorporation of work + retest
  • Testing at all levels: System, unit, integration, smoke, regression (prefer automated)
  • ATDD: Team defines acceptance criteria → creates tests → write minimum code
  • TDD / BDD: Write tests before product; mistake-proofing
  • Spikes: Timeboxed research for estimation, acceptance criteria, user flow understanding

5.2.8 Iterations + Increments → Working Product

  • Iterations create cadence for delivery and feedback
  • Increments provide value for delivery and feedback
  • Demo → feedback → retrospect → adapt → next iteration
  • Schedule demo appropriate for delivery cadence

5.3 Troubleshooting Agile Project Challenges

Unclear purpose/mission
Agile chartering — vision, mission, mission tests
Unclear working agreements
Chartering for alignment — values, principles, working agreements
Unclear requirements
Product vision, roadmap, user story mapping, impact mapping; PO clarifies
Poor user experience
UX design practices included in team; involve users early and often
Inaccurate estimation
Reduce story size; relative estimation with whole team; agile modeling; spikes
Unclear work assignments
Kanban boards; daily standup to walk board
Team stuck on obstacles
Servant leader clears obstacles; escalate if needed
Insufficient backlog refinement
PO + team workshop stories; Definition of Ready; split into smaller stories
Defects
Pair work, collective ownership, pervasive testing, robust Definition of Done
Technical debt
Refactoring, agile modeling, automated testing, Definition of Done
Too much product complexity
"Simplest thing that works" principle; maximize work NOT done
Slow process improvement
No more than 3 improvement items per retrospective; focus on completing each
Too much upfront work/rework
Spikes to learn instead; shorten iterations; robust Definition of Done
Rush/wait uneven flow
Plan to capacity; stop multitasking; pairs/swarm/mob to even capabilities
Siloed teams
Self-organize as cross-functional; use servant leadership to educate managers

5.4 Measurements in Agile Projects

⚠️ Watermelon Project Anti-Pattern
Green on outside (traffic light reporting says OK), Red on inside (project is behind). Problem with predictive measurement: no empirical data until too late. Agile favors empirical, value-based measurements = finished work, not predicted work.
Velocity
Story points completed per iteration. Used to forecast future iterations. 4-8 iterations to stabilize velocity.
Lead Time
Total time from item added to board → delivered to customer. Measures end-to-end flow.
Cycle Time
Time to process an item (start → complete). Measures team throughput; identifies bottlenecks.
Burndown
Work REMAINING vs. time. Shows risk early. Team can see by Day 3 if at risk. Can be dispiriting.
Burnup
Work COMPLETED vs. time. Shows what was accomplished. Also shows scope changes clearly.
Cumulative Flow Diagram (CFD)
Work in progress across all board columns. Wide band in any column = bottleneck. Adapt to project task board.
EVM in Agile
SPI = Story points completed / Story points planned
CPI = Earned Value (completed features value) / Actual Costs
If team planned 30 pts, completed 25 → SPI = 25/30 = 0.83 (83% of planned rate)
Story Points vs. Completed Stories
Measuring STORY POINTS ≠ Measuring COMPLETED STORIES/FEATURES.
Primary measure = working software/product (Principle #7).
Story points = relative estimation; NOT comparable between teams.

Organizational Considerations for Project Agility

6.1 Organizational Change Management

OCM covers skills/techniques for influencing changes that support agility. For full OCM guidance: PMI's Managing Change in Organizations: A Practice Guide.

6.1.1 Drivers for Change Management

  • Accelerated delivery changes: Receiving organization may not be ready to absorb outputs at increased pace. Customer acceptance critical.
  • Agile adoption changes: More frequent handoffs, rework viewed negatively, transition friction requires OCM techniques.

6.1.2 Change-Friendly Characteristics

  • Executive management's willingness to change
  • Willingness to shift performance reviews
  • Centralization/decentralization of PM functions
  • Short-term budgeting vs. long-term goals balance
  • Talent management maturity

Roadblocks to Organizational Agility

  • Work decomposed into departmental silos
  • Procurement based on short-term pricing (not competency)
  • Leaders rewarded for local efficiency (not end-to-end flow)
  • Specialized contributors with no incentive to diversify
  • Decentralized portfolios: employees on too many projects

Accelerating Cultural Compatibility

  • Visible and active executive sponsorship
  • OCM: communication and coaching
  • Progressive pacing of agile adoption project-by-project
  • Incremental introduction of agile practices
  • Leading by example

6.2 Organizational Culture

"Culture eats strategy for breakfast" — Peter Drucker
6.2.1 Environment of Safety
Most important cultural norm: safe, honest, transparent environment. Only in safety can teams reflect on successes and apply lessons from failures without fear.
6.2.2 Culture Assessment Axes
Exploration ↔ Execution
Speed ↔ Stability
Quantity ↔ Quality
Flexibility ↔ Predictability
Use to choose right agile techniques.

6.3 Procurement and Contracts

Agile Manifesto on Procurement
"Customer collaboration OVER contract negotiation." Pursue shared-risk-reward relationship where ALL sides win (not winners vs. losers).

6.4 Business Practices

As cross-functional teams deliver value, they challenge internal support functions:

  • Finance: May capitalize products differently when delivered incrementally
  • Procurement: Contracts may need restructuring for frequent delivery
  • HR: Individual incentives may conflict with team-based self-organizing culture
  • Management: Performance appraisals of self-organizing employees need rethinking

6.5 Multiteam Coordination — Scaling

Key Rule
If using agile for a single team is NOT successful → do NOT try to scale. Fix the foundational team issues first. Healthy agile team is a critical success factor.

Scaling frameworks: SAFe®, LeSS, Disciplined Agile (DA), Scrum of Scrums. More details in Annex A3.

Scaling Approach
Start small → learn what works → scale gradually. Apply agile thinking to adjust existing program management. Healthy single-team agile is prerequisite to scaling.

6.6 Agile and the PMO

6.6.1 Value-Driven PMO

  • Operates like consulting business
  • Tailors to specific project needs
  • Tools/templates for some; executive coaching for others
  • Intrapreneur approach — focus on most valuable activities

6.6.2 Invitation-Oriented PMO

  • Invite (not mandate) engagement with PMO services
  • Higher engagement = "stickier" practices
  • If delivering value → clients will request services naturally

6.6.3 Multidisciplinary PMO

  • Agile center of excellence services:
  • Standards (templates, tools, education)
  • Training/mentoring → agile mindset
  • Multiproject management coordination
  • Facilitating organizational learning
  • Managing stakeholders (PO training)
  • Recruiting/evaluating team leaders

6.7 Organizational Structure Factors

FactorImpact on Agility
Geography (distributed)Competing goals, language barriers, lower visibility. Use agile to encourage more collaboration. Tailor techniques.
Functionalized structuresGeneral resistance to cross-functional collaboration. Work with managers to allocate people.
Size of deliverableSmaller deliverables → more frequent handoffs → faster value flow

Procurement-heavyVendors leave with project knowledge. Use retrospectives while vendor engaged.

6.8 Evolving the Organization

Treat Change as an Agile Project
Create a backlog of organizational changes, prioritized by value. Each change = experiment tested for short period. Use Kanban boards to track: To-Do → In-Progress → Done. Rolling out changes transparently improves likelihood of success.
A3
Overview of Agile & Lean Frameworks (Annex A3)
Framework Selection Criteria in This Guide
Selected frameworks must be: (1) Designed for holistic use, (2) Formalized for common use, (3) Popular in modern use (measured by industry surveys). Custom company methods and niche frameworks are OUT of scope.
TEAM — ITERATION-BASED

Scrum

  • Single-team framework; sprints of ≤1 month
  • Roles: Product Owner (maximizes value) + Development Team (cross-functional, self-organizing) + Scrum Master (upholds process, removes impediments, coaches)
  • Events: Sprint → Sprint Planning → Daily Scrum → Sprint Review → Sprint Retrospective
  • Artifacts: Product Backlog + Sprint Backlog + Increment
  • Each sprint = potentially releasable increment
TEAM — TECHNICAL PRACTICES

eXtreme Programming (XP)

  • Distills best practices to purest form, applies continuously
  • Core Values: Communication, Simplicity, Feedback, Courage, Respect
  • Organizational: Sit together, whole team, informative workspace
  • Technical: Pair programming, test-first, incremental design, shared code
  • Planning: User stories, weekly cycle, quarterly cycle, slack
  • Integration: 10-min build, continuous integration, test-first
TEAM — FLOW-BASED

Kanban Method

  • Inspired by Toyota lean manufacturing (1953, Taiichi Ohno)
  • "Visual sign" — boards with columns representing workflow states
  • Start-where-you-are approach; least disruptive to implement
  • No prescribed timeboxes; continuous flow
  • Defining Principles: Start with current state, Agree to incremental evolutionary change, Respect current process/roles, Encourage leadership at all levels
  • Core Properties: Visualize workflow, Limit WIP, Manage flow, Make policies explicit, Implement feedback loops, Improve collaboratively
  • Best for: flexibility, continuous delivery, variable workload, focus, waste reduction
TEAM — SCALABLE BY SIZE/CRITICALITY

Crystal Methods

  • Family of methodologies scaled by team size AND project criticality
  • Colors: Crystal Clear (1-6 people) → Yellow → Orange → Red (up to 200+)
  • Criticality levels: Comfort → Discretionary Money → Essential Money → Life
  • Core Values: People, Interaction, Community, Skills, Talents, Communications
  • Common Properties: Frequent delivery, Reflective improvement, Close communication, Personal safety, Focus, Expert user access, Automated testing/CI/config mgmt
HYBRID — TRANSITION APPROACH

Scrumban

  • Designed as transition from Scrum to Kanban; now a hybrid framework
  • Scrum as framework + Kanban for process improvement
  • Short sprints + kanban board to visualize/monitor work
  • WIP limits to manage work
  • Planning trigger: when WIP drops below predetermined level
  • No predefined roles — team retains current roles
  • Daily meetings for collaboration and impediment removal
TEAM — FEATURE-CENTRIC

Feature-Driven Development (FDD)

  • For large software projects; feature = small business value capability
  • 6 Roles: Project Manager, Chief Architect, Development Manager, Chief Programmer, Class Owner, Domain Expert
  • 5 Activities (iterative): Develop overall model → Build features list → Plan by feature → Design by feature → Build by features
  • Best practices: Domain object modeling, feature teams, inspections, regular builds, visibility of progress
TEAM — CONSTRAINT-DRIVEN

Dynamic Systems Development Method (DSDM)

  • Noncommercial collaboration; adds rigor to iterative methods
  • Known for: constraint-driven delivery — Cost, Quality, Time are FIXED; Scope is VARIABLE
  • Traditional approach reverses this (scope fixed, others variable)
  • 8 Principles: Focus on business need, Deliver on time, Collaborate, Never compromise quality, Build incrementally from firm foundations, Develop iteratively, Communicate continuously, Demonstrate control
TEAM — UP VARIANT

Agile Unified Process (AgileUP)

  • Offshoot of RUP (Rational Unified Process); lighter weight
  • 7 disciplines with iterative cycles before formal delivery
  • Disciplines: Model, Implementation, Test, Deployment, Config Mgmt, Project Mgmt, Environment
  • Principles: Team knows what it's doing, Simplicity, Agility, Focus on high-value, Tool independence, Tailoring, Situationally specific
SCALING — TEAM OF TEAMS

Scrum of Scrums (SoS)

  • For 2+ Scrum teams needing coordination; also "meta Scrum"
  • One representative per team meets daily/2-3x week
  • Reports: Completed work + Next work + Current impediments + Potential blocking impediments for other teams
  • Can scale: Scrum of Scrum of Scrums for very large programs
SCALING — ENTERPRISE

Scaled Agile Framework (SAFe®)

  • Enterprise-level patterns for scaling agile
  • Organizes around value streams delivering continuous customer value
  • Levels: Team + Program + Portfolio
  • 9 Principles: Economic view, Systems thinking, Variability, Build incrementally with fast cycles, Milestone = objective evaluation, Visualize + limit WIP, Cadence + synchronization, Unlock intrinsic motivation, Decentralize decisions
SCALING — MINIMAL EXTENSION

Large Scale Scrum (LeSS)

  • Extends Scrum with minimal additions — preserves single-team Scrum essence
  • Same as Scrum: One product backlog, one Definition of Done, one increment/sprint, one PO, complete cross-functional teams, one sprint
  • LeSS Additions: Sprint planning in two parts (What+How), organic cross-team coordination, overall cross-team refinement, overall retrospective
  • Principles: Systems thinking, whole product focus, transparency
SCALING — PROCESS DECISION FRAMEWORK

Disciplined Agile (DA)

  • Integrates agile best practices; balanced between narrow (Scrum) and prescriptive (AgileUP)
  • Principles: People-first, Learning-oriented, Full delivery life cycle, Goal-driven, Enterprise awareness, Scalable
  • Promotes fit-for-purpose life cycles
  • Covers multiple dimensions of program complexity
  • Offers cross-departmental governance guidance

Exam-Focused Glossary — Key Terms & Definitions

Definitions directly from the Agile Practice Guide — focus on these for exam.

Agile
A mindset of values and principles as set forth in the Agile Manifesto; a blanket term for adaptive frameworks.
Agile Life Cycle
An approach that is both iterative AND incremental to refine work items and deliver frequently.
Agile Manifesto
The original and official definition of agile values and principles. Published 2001.
Agile Mindset
A way of thinking and behaving underpinned by the 4 values and 12 principles of the Agile Manifesto.
ATDD
Acceptance Test-Driven Development — collaboratively create acceptance tests BEFORE delivery begins.
Backlog Refinement
Progressive elaboration; ongoing activity where team reviews, updates, and writes requirements to satisfy customer need.
BDD
Behavior-Driven Development — system design and validation using test-first principles and English-like scripts.
Broken Comb / Paint Drip
A person with various DEPTHS of specialization in multiple skills. Contrast: T-shaped (one deep + broad), I-shaped (one deep only).
Burndown Chart
Graphical representation of work REMAINING vs. time left in timebox.
Burnup Chart
Graphical representation of work COMPLETED toward release. Shows scope changes clearly.
Cadence
A rhythm of execution. See also: Timebox.
Collective Code Ownership
Any team member authorized to modify any project work product — emphasizes team-wide accountability.
Continuous Delivery
Delivering feature increments immediately to customers, often through small batches and automation.
Continuous Integration
Each team member's work frequently integrated and validated with others.
Crystal
Family of lightweight agile methods scaled by team SIZE and project CRITICALITY.
Cycle Time
Time required to PROCESS an item (start work → complete). Identifies bottlenecks.
Daily Scrum / Standup
Brief (max 15 min) daily meeting. Team reviews yesterday, declares today's intent, highlights obstacles.
Definition of Done (DoD)
Team's checklist of all criteria required for a deliverable to be considered ready for customer use.
Definition of Ready (DoR)
Team's checklist ensuring a user story has all information needed for the team to begin work.
DevOps
Practices for smooth delivery flow by improving collaboration between development and operations staff.
Disciplined Agile (DA)
Process decision framework enabling simplified process decisions around incremental and iterative delivery.
DSDM
Dynamic Systems Development Method — constraint-driven agile framework. Cost/Quality/Time fixed; Scope variable.
Flow Master
Coach for a team in continuous flow/Kanban context. Equivalent to Scrum Master.
Hybrid Approach
Combination of two or more agile and non-agile elements, having a non-agile end result.
Impact Mapping
Strategic planning technique — roadmap to organization while building new products.
Impediment / Blocker
An obstacle that prevents the team from achieving its objectives.
Increment
A functional, tested, and accepted deliverable that is a SUBSET of the overall project outcome.
Incremental Life Cycle
Approach providing finished deliverables the customer may be able to use IMMEDIATELY.
Information Radiator
Visible physical display providing information without disturbing the team. E.g., Kanban board.
I-shaped
Person with a SINGLE deep specialization; no breadth. Not ideal for agile teams.
Iteration
A TIMEBOXED cycle of development in which all work needed to deliver value is performed.
Iterative Life Cycle
Approach allowing feedback on UNFINISHED work to improve and modify that work.
Kanban Board
Visualization tool making bottlenecks and work quantities visible; enables flow improvement.
Kanban Method
Agile method inspired by lean inventory control; specifically for knowledge work. "Start-where-you-are."
Lead Time
TOTAL time from item added to board → delivered to customer (includes queue time).
LeSS
Large Scale Scrum — extends Scrum with minimal additions; preserves single-team Scrum essence.
Mobbing
Multiple team members simultaneously focusing and coordinating on ONE particular work item.
Minimum Viable Product (MVP)
Smallest deliverable with enough features to satisfy early customers and collect feedback.
Organizational Bias
Organization's preferences on scales: Exploration↔Execution, Speed↔Stability, Quantity↔Quality, Flexibility↔Predictability.
Pair Work / Pair Programming
Two team members working simultaneously on the same work item (programming variant = pair programming).
Personas
Archetype user representing similar end users, described with goals, motivations, personal characteristics.
Pivot
A planned course correction to test a new hypothesis about the product or strategy.
Predictive Life Cycle
Traditional approach with bulk of planning UPFRONT, then executing in single pass. Sequential.
Product Backlog
An ORDERED list of user-centric requirements that a team maintains for a product.
Product Owner
Responsible for maximizing product value; ultimately accountable for end product built.
Progressive Elaboration
Iterative process of increasing detail in plans as more information and accurate estimates become available.
Refactoring
Improving product DESIGN (maintainability, other attributes) without altering expected behavior.
Retrospective
Regularly occurring workshop where participants explore their work/results to improve process and product.
Rolling Wave Planning
Iterative planning: near-term work planned in detail; future work planned at higher level.
SAFe®
Scaled Agile Framework — knowledge base of integrated patterns for enterprise-scale lean-agile development.
Scrum
Agile framework for developing complex products. Specific roles (PO, Dev Team, SM), events (Sprint, Planning, Daily, Review, Retrospective), artifacts (Product Backlog, Sprint Backlog, Increment).
Scrumban
Management framework using Scrum as the way of working + Kanban Method as improvement lens.
Scrum Master
Coach of dev team; process owner. Removes obstacles, facilitates events, defends team from disruptions.
Scrum of Scrums (SoS)
Technique for multiple Scrum teams; one representative per team meets to coordinate interdependencies.
Self-Organizing Team
Cross-functional team where people FLUIDLY assume leadership as needed to achieve objectives.
Servant Leadership
Leading through SERVICE to the team; focusing on understanding and addressing needs of team members.
Siloed Organization
Organization structured to contribute only a SUBSET of aspects required for delivering value to customers.
Smoke Testing
Lightweight set of tests ensuring the MOST IMPORTANT functions work as intended — first-look test.
Specification by Example (SBE)
Collaborative approach defining requirements using REALISTIC EXAMPLES instead of abstract statements.
Spike
Short timeboxed interval for RESEARCH or PROTOTYPING to prove viability; for estimation/acceptance criteria.
Sprint
A timeboxed ITERATION in Scrum (≤1 month). Produces a potentially releasable increment.
Story Point
A UNIT-LESS measure used in RELATIVE user story estimation. Not comparable between teams.
Swarming
Multiple team members collectively focusing on resolving a SPECIFIC IMPEDIMENT.
Technical Debt
The DEFERRED cost of work not done at an earlier point in the product life cycle.
TDD
Test-Driven Development — tests defined BEFORE work begins; validates continuously; zero-defect mindset.
Timebox
A FIXED period of time (e.g., 1 week, 1 month). See also: Iteration.
T-shaped
Person with ONE deep specialization + BROAD ability across other required skills. IDEAL for agile teams.
User Story
Brief description of deliverable value for a specific user. A PROMISE for a conversation to clarify details.
User Story Mapping
Visual practice organizing work to understand high-value features, identify backlog omissions, plan releases.
Value Stream
Organizational construct focusing on FLOW of value to customers through specific products/services.
Velocity
Sum of story point sizes for features ACTUALLY completed in an iteration. Used for future planning.
WIP (Work in Progress)
Work started but not yet completed. WIP limits prevent overloading team and expose bottlenecks.
XP (eXtreme Programming)
Software development method based on frequent cycles; distills best practices to purest form. Origins of TDD, pair programming, CI, refactoring for agile.
