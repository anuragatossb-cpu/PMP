Agile was developed for software but applies to any project with high uncertainty. It builds, delivers, and learns in small cycles rather than doing everything upfront and hoping for the best.

Individuals & Interactions over Processes & Tools:
  • Projects are undertaken by people, not tools
  • Problems get solved by people, not processes
  • Projects are ultimately about people

  • Plans are living documents, not contracts

12 Agile Guiding Principles ⭐⭐ MUST MEMORISE
"The 12 principles translate the 4 values into actionable behaviours"
▼
Principles 1–6:
  1. Satisfy the customer through early and continuous delivery of valuable software
  2. Welcome changing requirements, even late in development — agile harnesses change for competitive advantage
  3. Deliver working software frequently — weeks to months, prefer shorter timescales
  4. Business people and developers work together daily throughout the project
  5. Build around motivated individuals — give them environment and trust them
  6. Face-to-face conversation is the most efficient communication method
Principles 7–12:
  1. Working software is the primary measure of progress
  2. Sustainable development — maintain constant pace indefinitely
  3. Continuous attention to technical excellence enhances agility
  4. Simplicity — maximise the amount of work NOT done is essential
  5. Best architectures emerge from self-organising teams
  6. At regular intervals, team reflects and adjusts behaviour (retrospectives)
Exam Bullets — Manifesto & Principles
  • The Agile Manifesto has 4 values and 12 principles — created in 2001
  • The RIGHT side still has value — Agile doesn't eliminate processes, docs, or contracts
  • Principle 7: Working software is the primary measure of progress (not % complete)
  • Principle 10: Simplicity — maximise the work NOT done. Less is more.
  • Principle 12: Retrospectives are mandated by the Agile Manifesto itself
  • Principle 1: Customer satisfaction through early and CONTINUOUS delivery is the highest priority

S1
Scrum — 3 Pillars ⭐ EXAM HEAVY
"Transparency → Inspection → Adaptation — each pillar enables the next"
▼
"

Scrum is not just a process — it's a framework built on three empirical principles. Without transparency you can't inspect. Without inspection you can't adapt. Each pillar depends on the one before.

🔍 Transparency

Visibility to those responsible for the outcome. Everyone can see what is happening. Sprint progress, backlog status, Definition of Done — all visible to all.

🔎 Inspection

Timely checks on how well the project is progressing toward goals. Looks for problematic deviations or differences from goals. Happens at every Scrum event.

🔄 Adaptation

Adjusting a process to minimise further issues when an inspection shows a problem or undesirable trend. Retrospectives and Sprint Reviews are the primary adaptation events.

Increment
Review + Retro

Daily Scrum happens every day within the Sprint. The cycle then repeats with the next Sprint.

S2
Scrum Roles — 3 Roles ⭐⭐ EXAM CRITICAL
"Product Owner owns WHAT. Scrum Master owns HOW. Dev Team does the WORK."
▼
🎯 Product Owner:
  • Owns the product vision
  • Defines features; decides release date and content
  • Responsible for market success
  • Prioritises features by business value
  • Can change features and priorities every Sprint
  • Manages and grooms the Product Backlog
  • Defines the Definition of Done
  • Attends all Scrum events
🛡️ Scrum Master:
  • Responsible for facilitating the Scrum process
  • Shields the team from external interruptions
  • Removes impediments to progress
  • Looks for ways to enhance productivity
  • Acts as servant leader
  • Coaches the team on Scrum practices
  • Facilitates all Scrum events
  • = XP Coach, = Agile PM
⚙️ Development Team:
  • Small group with all necessary skills (cross-functional)
  • Focuses on steady delivery of high quality features
  • Generates options for delivery
  • Self-organising — manages own work within Sprints
  • Ideally 3-9 members
  • Writes acceptance tests
  • Demos completed increments
  • Does its own estimation
Exam Bullets — Scrum Roles
  • Product Owner = Customer in XP terminology — same role, different name
  • Scrum Master = Coach in XP terminology — servant leader, not boss
  • The Scrum Master does NOT manage the team — the team is self-organising
  • Product Owner can change priorities every Sprint but NOT during a Sprint
  • Dev Team size: 3-9 members is ideal. Too small = limited skills; too large = coordination overhead

Daily Scrum — 3 Questions:
  1. What did I do yesterday?
  2. What will I do today?
  3. Are there any impediments in my way?

Same time, same place every day. Led by Scrum Master. 15 minutes — no more.

Sprint Retrospective Topics:
  • What went well? (keep doing)
  • What went wrong? (stop doing)
  • What to do more of?
  • What to do less of?

= Lessons Learned for the Sprint. Happens AFTER the Sprint Review and BEFORE the next Sprint Planning.

⚠️ Sprint Rules — Critical!
  • During the sprint, no changes are made that would affect the sprint goal
  • Development team members are kept the same throughout the sprint
  • Sprint can be cancelled by Product Owner only — if sprint goal becomes obsolete
  • Timeboxed = fixed duration; unused time is not extended
S4
Scrum Artifacts — 3 Artifacts + DoD ⭐⭐ EXAM CRITICAL
"Backlog → Backlog → Increment — each sprint creates a potentially shippable product"
▼
📋 Product Backlog:
  • Prioritised list of all work needed to complete the product
  • Owned and prioritised by the Product Owner
  • Sorted by value — most valuable items listed first
  • Dynamic — constantly evolves and is refined
  • Team and PO "groom the backlog" (refine/estimate)
  • Never complete until product is retired
📝 Sprint Backlog:
  • Items from Product Backlog selected for this Sprint
  • Accompanied by a plan for how to achieve the sprint goal
  • Only updated by the Development Team
  • Highly visible view of work being done
  • Dev team's forecast for the Sprint
  • Fixed once Sprint starts (unless cancelled)
📦 Product Increment:
  • Part of the product that is done after each Sprint
  • Must meet the Definition of Done
  • Potentially shippable — usable by end users
  • Done to gather feedback after each Sprint
  • Each increment is additive to prior increments
⭐ Definition of Done (DoD)

Shared understanding of what "done" means. Defined at project start, applies to all work. May include: unit tests pass, code reviewed, documentation updated, integration tests pass. Prevents misunderstanding between team and Product Owner.

DoD is defined for: User Stories · Sprint Releases · Final Product

Exam Bullets — Scrum Artifacts
  • Product Backlog items are prioritised by business value — not technical priority
  • Sprint Backlog can ONLY be updated by the Development Team — not PO or SM
  • The Product Increment must meet the Definition of Done to count as complete
  • Backlog Grooming / Refinement = adding detail, estimates, and ordering to items
  • Velocity = average story points completed per Sprint — used for release planning

Extreme Programming (XP)

Software-development centric Agile method focusing on technical practices and good coding standards.

5 Values 4 Roles 12+ Practices
X1
XP Values, Roles & Key Practices ⭐ EXAM HEAVY
"Scrum manages the project; XP manages the technical work inside it"
▼
"

XP and Scrum complement each other — Scrum provides the project framework while XP provides the engineering practices. They are often used together.

5 XP Core Values:
  • Simplicity — Find the simplest thing that could possibly work. Remove complexity.
  • Communication — Team members know what is expected; daily standup is key
  • Feedback — Get impressions of correctness early; failing fast allows faster improvement
  • Courage — Allow work to be entirely visible to others; honest conversations
  • Respect — Everyone is accountable for success or failure; recognise differences
4 XP Roles:
  • Coach — Mentor; guides process; facilitator. = Scrum Master
  • Customer — Business representative; provides requirements and priorities. = Product Owner
  • Programmers — Developers who build the product; write the code
  • Testers — Help customer define and write acceptance tests for user stories

  • Refactoring = improvement without changing external behaviour; keeps code clean and maintainable
  • XP focuses on technical excellence; Scrum focuses on project management — they complement each other

Kanban Development

Derived from Toyota's lean production system. "Kanban" means "signboard" in Japanese. Visualise and limit work.

5 Principles Limit WIP No fixed iterations
K1
Kanban Principles, Board & WIP Limits ⭐ EXAM HEAVY
"Visualise work, limit what's in progress, manage the flow — pull, don't push"
▼
"

Kanban doesn't prescribe sprints or roles — it's a flow-based system. Work items are pulled when capacity exists rather than pushed onto the team. The board makes ALL work visible to ALL stakeholders at all times.

5 Kanban Core Principles:
  1. Visualise the workflow — make invisible knowledge work visible using a board
  2. Limit WIP — keeping work in progress low increases visibility of issues and bottlenecks
  3. Manage flow — track work through the system; identify issues; measure for effectiveness
  4. Make process policies explicit — clearly explain how things work for open discussions
  5. Improve collaboration — through scientific measurement and experimentation

Can be physical (whiteboard) or digital. Acts as "information radiator" — anyone can see status at a glance.

⭐ WIP Limits & Cycle Time
Cycle Time = WIP / Throughput (Little's Law) Example: Team completes 5 items/day, 30 items in progress Cycle Time = 30 / 5 = 6 days per item Reducing WIP → Reduces Cycle Time → Faster delivery!

Excessive WIP hides bottlenecks, represents money with no return, and increases risk of rework. Always limit WIP.

Cumulative Flow Diagram (CFD):

Stack graph showing how work progresses through stages over time. Widening bands indicate bottlenecks. Used to track flow efficiency, spot bottlenecks, and forecast completion. Theory of Constraints — identify and resolve the bottleneck activity.

Lean Software Development

Originated from Toyota's manufacturing system (TPS). Applied to software development. Eliminate waste, deliver fast.

7 Principles 7 Wastes Toyota Origin
L1
Lean Principles & 7 Wastes ⭐ EXAM HEAVY
"Maximise value, minimise waste — deliver fast by removing everything that doesn't add value"
▼
"

Lean asks one question about every activity: does this create value for the customer? If not, it's waste. Eliminate waste relentlessly to deliver faster with less cost.

7 Lean Principles:
  1. Eliminate Waste — partially done work, delays, handoffs, unnecessary features
  2. Amplify Learning — communicate early and often; get feedback quickly; build on learning
  3. Defer Decisions — commit to things as late as possible; keep options open
  4. Deliver Fast — quickly deliver valuable software; iterate through designs
  5. Empower the Team — respect team's technical knowledge; let them make technical decisions
  6. Build Quality In — build quality into the product; assure quality throughout development
  7. Optimise the Whole — see the system as more than the sum of its parts
7 Wastes of Lean (TIMWOOD + P):
  1. Partially done work — started but not complete; no value delivered
  2. Extra processes — unnecessary approval steps, reviews, sign-offs
  3. Extra features — building things customers don't need
  4. Task switching — context switching between tasks reduces efficiency
  5. Waiting — waiting for approval, resources, or the next task
  6. Motion — unnecessary movement or handoffs
  7. Defects — rework caused by defects; cost increases the later they're found
💡 Value Stream Mapping

Technique to optimise flow of information or materials. Steps: (1) Identify product/service, (2) Map current state value stream, (3) Review to find waste, (4) Map future state with improvements, (5) Develop roadmap to implement, (6) Revisit regularly. Goal: reduce waiting time and eliminate non-value-adding activities.

"

Predictive does everything once. Iterative repeats until correct. Incremental delivers in parts. Agile combines iterative and incremental to deliver frequent small releases that continuously improve.

⭐ PMI Agile Practice Guide — Table 3.1

Key insight: Agile leverages BOTH aspects of Iterative (repeated until correct) AND Incremental (frequent smaller deliveries). This combination maximises customer value and feedback.

Definable Work (use Predictive):
  • Clear, well-understood procedures
  • Proved successful on similar past projects
  • Low rates of change and uncertainty
  • Examples: car production, electrical appliances, construction
High Uncertainty Work (use Agile):
  • Not-done-before type of work
  • High rates of change, complexity, and risk
  • Requirements unclear or likely to change
  • Examples: software development, designing a new product, R&D

Domain II

Value-Driven Delivery

Agile projects exist to generate business value. Deliver highest-value items first, early, and often.

V1
Value, Prioritisation & MVP ⭐⭐ EXAM CRITICAL
"Deliver the highest value first — a happy stakeholder is a successful project"
▼
"

Value-based prioritisation means putting the most valuable features at the top of the backlog and delivering them first. Every sprint should be answering: "What does the customer value most right now?"

Prioritisation Techniques — All Must Be Known:
TechniqueHow It Works
Simple SchemePriority 1, 2, 3. Risk: everything becomes Priority 1.
MoSCoWMust Have / Should Have / Could Have / Won't Have this time
Monopoly MoneyGive everyone equal monopoly money; distribute to what they value most
100-Point MethodEach person gets 100 points to distribute across requirements
Dot VotingEach person gets dots to place on requirements they support
Kano AnalysisCategorises features: Delighters (excite), Satisfiers (expected), Dissatisfiers (must have), Indifferent
Req. Prioritisation ModelCombines value, cost, risk, and dependencies
Financial Metrics for Value:
  • ROI — Return on Investment: benefits received / money invested (percentage)
  • IRR — Internal Rate of Return: interest rate needed to break even in today's money
  • NPV — Net Present Value: value of future money in today's terms
  • EVM — Earned Value Management: monitors value of project as it progresses (CPI, SPI)
Minimal Viable Product (MVP):

A set of functionality that is complete enough to be useful but small enough not to be an entire project. Usually a module or subset. Delivered early to get real feedback from real users.

⭐ MoSCoW — Most Tested Prioritisation Technique
  • Must Have — Critical requirements; project fails without them
  • Should Have — Important but not critical; can be deferred
  • Could Have — Nice to have; included if time permits
  • Won't Have (this time) — Agreed to be excluded from this release
Agile Contracting:
  • Agile fixes resources and time; varies functionality
  • Graduated Fixed Price — buyer/seller share risks; different rates for early/on-time/late
  • Fixed Price Work Packages — mitigate under/over-estimating
  • "Money for nothing and change for free" principle
Key Performance Indicators (KPIs):
  • Rate of progress — how many story points completed
  • Remaining work — how much left in backlog
  • Likely completion date — based on velocity
  • Likely cost remaining — based on burn rate
Exam Bullets — Value-Driven Delivery
  • Deliver highest-value features FIRST — this reduces risk and builds stakeholder confidence
  • MVP = minimum set of features to be useful — get real feedback as early as possible
  • MoSCoW: Must, Should, Could, Won't — the most commonly tested prioritisation technique
  • Working software = primary measure of progress (Principle 7). NOT % complete or docs produced.
  • Risk is anti-value — it has the potential to remove or reduce value. Manage it aggressively.

Domain III

Stakeholder Engagement

Projects are done by people for people. Active, continuous stakeholder engagement is a cornerstone of Agile.

ST1
Stakeholder Engagement, Vision & Communication ⭐ EXAM HEAVY
"Short iterations keep stakeholders engaged — engagement builds trust and drives success"
▼
"

In Agile, stakeholders aren't just consulted at milestones — they are active participants throughout. Sprint Reviews, demos, and daily collaboration keep them informed and invested in the outcome.

Methods of Stakeholder Engagement:
  • Get the right stakeholders — identify and include all relevant parties
  • Cement stakeholder involvement — make their participation essential
  • Actively manage stakeholder interest throughout the project
  • Frequently discuss what "done" looks like
  • Show progress and capabilities at Sprint Reviews
  • Candidly discuss estimates and projections
Set a Shared Vision — Tools:
  • Agile Charter — high-level agreement; uses W5H (Who, What, When, Where, Why, How)
  • Definition of Done — shared understanding of completion criteria
  • Wireframes — quick mock-ups ("low-fidelity prototyping"); clarify what done looks like
  • Personas — profiles of key users; goal-oriented; help team focus on valuable features
  • Use Case Diagrams — visually shows how users use the application
  • Data Models — how data is structured and related
  • Project Tweet — describe project goal in 140 characters or less

⭐ Team Tracking Charts — Burn Up, Burn Down, Velocity
Burndown Chart:

Shows remaining work over time. Line goes DOWN toward zero. Goal: reach zero by Sprint end. If line is above expected — behind schedule. Used DAILY.

Domain VI

Problem Detection & Resolution

Detect problems early when they're cheap to fix. Technical debt, defects, and variance analysis.

PD1
Technical Debt, Defects & Lead/Cycle Time ⭐ EXAM HEAVY
"The cost of change rises over time — find defects early or pay exponentially more later"
▼
"

Every day you leave a defect unfixed, more code gets built on top of it. Technical debt compounds like financial debt — the longer you wait, the more it costs to repay.

Technical Debt:

Backlog of work caused by not doing regular cleanup and refactoring. Accumulates when shortcuts are taken. If not addressed, leads to increased cost of development and makes it harder to implement future changes. Refactoring is the solution.

Cost of Change Rule:

The cost of fixing a defect increases dramatically the later in the development cycle it is discovered. Fix in development: cheap. Fix in testing: moderate. Fix in production: expensive. Fix after customer finds it: most expensive. Agile's frequent iterations catch defects early.

📐 Lead Time vs. Cycle Time
Cycle Time = WIP / Throughput (Little's Law) Lead Time = Total time from request to delivery (end-to-end) Cycle Time = Time to process ONE item through the system (part of lead time) Example: Feature requires 60 points, team does 5 points/day Cycle Time = 60 / 5 = 12 days

Excessive WIP → longer cycle time → money spent with no return. Limit WIP to reduce cycle time.

Variance & Trend Analysis:
  • Variance — measures how far actual performance is from expected
  • Trend Analysis — provides insight into future issues based on pattern
  • Lagging Metrics — measure what already happened (e.g., defects found)
  • Leading Metrics — predict what is about to happen (e.g., velocity trend)
  • Control Limits — diagnose issues before they occur; provide guidelines
Escaped Defects:

Defects that make it all the way to the customer. Most expensive type of defect. Agile's continuous testing and incremental delivery dramatically reduce escaped defects by catching them in early sprints before they compound.

Risk-Adjusted Backlog:

Adjusting the backlog order to account for risk — high-risk items may be pulled forward to resolve uncertainty early.

Retrospectives, PDCA & Process Improvement ⭐⭐ EXAM CRITICAL
"Retrospectives are the engine of continuous improvement — inspect and adapt every Sprint"
▼
"

Kaizen — Japanese for "continuous improvement" — is the philosophy behind every retrospective. Small, frequent improvements compound over time into significant team and process transformation.

⭐ Retrospective — 5 Stages (2-Hour Format)
StageTimeActivitiesPurpose
1. Set the Stage~6 minCheck-In, Focus On/Off, ESVP (Explorer/Shopper/Vacationer/Prisoner)Get everyone focused and participating

Also called the Agile Cycle: Plan → Develop → Evaluate → Learn → (repeat). Used in Kaizen and as the foundation of all retrospectives.

Short Subjects (Decide What to Do):
  • Start doing — new practices to adopt next Sprint
  • Stop doing — practices that are wasting time or causing harm
  • Do more of — practices that are working well
  • Do less of — practices with diminishing returns
SMART Goals:
  • Specific · Measurable · Attainable · Relevant · Timely
Process Tailoring:

Amend the methodology to better fit the project environment. Change things for good reason — not just for the sake of change. This is how teams develop their own hybrid approach. Key question: "Does this process add value for us?"

Pre-Mortems:

Team meeting BEFORE the project that looks at what could cause failure. Steps: (1) Think about possible failures, (2) Create list of causes, (3) Review project plan to reduce/remove those causes. Proactive risk identification tool.

Exam Bullets — Continuous Improvement
  • Retrospective = after every Sprint; ~2 hours; inspect process and create improvement plan
  • Five Whys = ask "why?" five times to reach root cause
  • PDCA = Plan-Do-Check-Act (Deming Cycle) — the foundation of Kaizen and all retrospectives
  • Kaizen focuses on small incremental improvements — not big bang changes
  • Process Tailoring — adapt the methodology; this is how hybrid approaches are created
  • Value Stream Mapping — find and eliminate waste in the process flow; reduce wait times
