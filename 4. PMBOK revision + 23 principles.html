D1 — Stakeholder Performance Domain
PMBOK Guide §2.1
📚 PMBOK 7 Guide §2.1 · Anurag Shrivastava — Licensed Copy
📌 Definition
Addresses activities and functions associated with stakeholders throughout the project life cycle. Effective execution yields: productive relationships, stakeholder agreement on objectives, project advocates, and satisfied stakeholders at closure.
1Identification and Analysis▼

Stakeholders must be identified continuously — new stakeholders emerge throughout the project. Anyone directly or indirectly affected by the project is a stakeholder.

Analysis Tools

ToolHow It WorksKey Exam Point
Power/Interest Grid2×2 matrix: High Power+High Interest=Manage Closely; High Power+Low Interest=Keep Satisfied; Low Power+High Interest=Keep Informed; Low Power+Low Interest=MonitorMost used analysis grid on the exam
Power/Influence GridSame as above but replaces Interest with Influence (ability to affect outcomes)Influence ≠ Interest
Salience Model3 dimensions: Power + Legitimacy + Urgency. All 3=Definitive (highest priority). Any 2=Secondary. Any 1=Latent.Definitive stakeholders = all 3 attributes
Stakeholder Cube3D model adding a third axis (e.g., attitude) to grid analysisMore nuanced than 2D grids
Empathy MapUnderstand stakeholder perspective: Think/Feel, Hear, See, Say/Do, Pain, GainUsed for deeper engagement planning

Stakeholder Engagement Levels

  • Unaware: No knowledge of project or its potential impacts on them
  • Resistant: Aware but opposed — resistant to change. Requires most engagement effort.
  • Neutral: Aware but neither supportive nor resistant
  • Supportive: Aware and supportive of change. Maintain this level.
  • Leading: Actively engaged in ensuring project success. Leverage these stakeholders.
⭐ EXAM
Gap between Current and Desired engagement level = the engagement action needed. Never leave Resistant stakeholders unaddressed — resistance is a project risk.
2Engagement Strategy and Communication▼
Communication MethodDescriptionBest For
InteractiveReal-time two-way: meetings, calls, video conferenceComplex discussions, sensitive issues, relationship building
PushSent from sender to receiver: email, reports, memos. No confirmation of receipt.Routine updates, large audiences, formal notification
PullReceiver accesses at will: intranet, repositories, e-learningOptional reference material, large audiences
💡 PMBOK §2.1 Key Insight
Stakeholder engagement is MORE than communication. Engagement involves two-way dialogue that builds shared understanding, not just information transmission. A project that communicates well but engages poorly will still fail stakeholder satisfaction.

Outcomes Check (PMBOK Table 2-1)

  • Productive working relationship with stakeholders throughout project life cycle
  • Stakeholder agreement on project requirements and what constitutes success
  • Stakeholders who are project advocates and supporters
  • Satisfied stakeholders at project end and benefits realisation stage

D2 — Team Performance Domain
PMBOK Guide §2.2
📚 PMBOK 7 Guide §2.2
📌 Definition
Addresses activities associated with the people responsible for producing project deliverables that realise business outcomes. Effective execution yields: shared ownership, high-performing team, applicable leadership, team members who demonstrate trust and respect.
1Project Team Management vs Leadership▼
Centralised Teams
Team members work in same location. Face-to-face communication is easiest. Easier to build team culture. Less flexibility for individual members.
Distributed/Virtual Teams
Members in different locations, time zones, languages. Require explicit communication norms. Technology bridges the gap. Risk of isolation and miscommunication.

Team Authority — 3 Key Concepts

  • Authority: Right to apply resources, make decisions, sign approvals. Formally granted. Can be delegated.
  • Accountability: Answerable for the outcome of a decision or work. CANNOT be delegated — always rests with one person.
  • Responsibility: Obligation to perform work or fulfil a task. CAN be shared among multiple team members.
⭐ EXAM TRAP
Accountability CANNOT be shared or delegated. Only one person is accountable for any outcome. Responsibility CAN be shared. This distinction appears frequently in scenario questions.
2Leadership Styles and Situational Approach▼
📝 PMBOK §2.2.1.2
"Effective project managers evaluate each situation and use the most applicable leadership style. In moments of chaos, directive action can create more clarity. For highly competent staff in a familiar environment, empowered leadership can elicit more productivity."
StyleWhen PM Uses ItTeam Development Level
Directing (S1)New team members, crisis, safety situation, very low team competenceD1 — Enthusiastic Beginner (Low comp, High commit)
Coaching (S2)Team learning but losing motivation, needs explanation and encouragementD2 — Disillusioned Learner (Low-Med comp, Low commit)
Supporting (S3)Capable team lacking confidence, needs encouragement not directionD3 — Capable but Cautious (Med-High comp, Variable)
Delegating (S4)Highly competent, motivated, self-organising teamD4 — Self-Reliant Achiever (High comp, High commit)

Servant Leadership (PMBOK Emphasis)

  • Serve the team — remove obstacles, provide resources, enable delivery
  • Protect the team from distractions and unnecessary interruptions
  • Coach team members for growth and skill development
  • Encourage collaborative decision-making
  • Foster psychological safety — team can speak up, fail safely, and recover
3Team Development, Conflict, Motivation▼

Tuckman Model (PMBOK §2.2.3.1)

StageTeam BehaviourPM Action
FormingPolite, uncertain, testing boundaries, dependent on PMDirecting — set structure, create charter, define roles
StormingConflict, competition, challenging authority — NORMALCoaching — mediate, enforce ground rules, allow healthy conflict
NormingCohesion forms, norms accepted, collaboration increasesSupporting — reinforce norms, celebrate wins
PerformingHigh productivity, self-organising, high trustDelegating — step back, focus on strategy and external
AdjourningDisbanding — emotions around endings, prideCelebrate, capture lessons, help team transition

Conflict Resolution (Ranked Best to Worst)

  • Confront/Problem-Solve: Direct discussion, win-win. ALWAYS try first.
  • Compromise/Reconcile: Each party gives something. Temporary solution.
  • Smooth/Accommodate: Emphasise agreements, one party yields. Short-term.
  • Force/Direct: PM imposes. Win-lose. Emergency/safety only.
  • Withdraw/Avoid: Never permanent. Cooling down only — always follow up.

Motivation — Key Theories

Maslow
Physio→Safety→Social→Esteem→Self-Actualisation. Lower before higher. Cannot skip levels.
Herzberg
Hygiene (salary, conditions) = prevent dissatisfaction only. Motivators (achievement, growth) = genuinely motivate. Raise ≠ motivator!
McGregor X/Y
X=workers lazy, need control. Y=self-motivated, seek responsibility. PMP assumes Theory Y.
McClelland
nAch (excel), nAff (relationships), nPow (influence). Institutional power = positive. Personal power = negative.

D3 — Development Approach and Life Cycle
PMBOK Guide §2.3
📚 PMBOK 7 Guide §2.3
📌 Definition
Addresses activities associated with development approach, cadence, and life cycle phases of the project. Effective execution yields: development approach appropriate to project, project life cycle connecting deliverables to outcomes, development approach enabling stakeholder involvement at right points.
1Development Approaches — Complete Comparison▼
ApproachCharacteristicsBest WhenRequirements
PredictiveSequential. Plan-driven. Detailed upfront planning. Scope fixed. Changes controlled via CCB.Requirements well-defined, stable technology, low uncertainty, regulatory/compliance projectsFixed from start
IterativeRepeat cycles to refine a SINGLE deliverable. Feedback improves the deliverable each cycle.Final product unclear, need multiple attempts to converge on right solutionEvolving
IncrementalDeliver in usable CHUNKS. Each increment adds functional capability. Customer uses each piece as delivered.Parts can be used before whole is complete, early value delivery neededCan be decomposed
AgileIterative + Incremental. Frequent feedback, rapid adaptation, self-organising teams, continuous delivery.High uncertainty, rapidly changing requirements, customer collaboration available dailyEmerging, evolving
HybridMix of predictive and agile. Predictive for stable parts (procurement, contracts), agile for development.Most real-world complex projects. Team or regulatory constraints prevent pure agile.Mixed
💡 KEY DISTINCTION
Iterative = improve THE SAME THING over multiple cycles. Incremental = add NEW PIECES. Agile = BOTH simultaneously. Building a car iteratively = redesign same car. Incrementally = add wheels, then engine, then body separately.

Factors Influencing Approach Selection (PMBOK §2.3.1)

Product/Deliverable Factors
Degree of innovation, technology certainty, requirements certainty, safety implications, regulatory requirements
Project Factors
Stakeholder engagement availability, team experience and capability, timeline constraints, budget flexibility
Organisational Factors
Organisational culture, PMO capability, tool availability, project team experience with approach

Delivery Cadence

  • Single Delivery: One final delivery at project end. Traditional waterfall. Construction, compliance projects.
  • Multiple Deliveries: Deliveries at regular or irregular intervals. Milestones. Most common in practice.
  • Periodic Delivery: Regular schedule-based delivery (weekly, monthly, quarterly releases).
  • Continuous Delivery: Continuous small releases as soon as features are ready. DevOps, CI/CD pipelines.

D4 — Planning Performance Domain
PMBOK Guide §2.4
📚 PMBOK 7 Guide §2.4
📌 Definition
Planning is NOT a one-time event — it is a continuous activity throughout the project. Effective execution yields: organised project progress, holistic delivery approach, progressive elaboration, appropriate planning effort, sufficient stakeholder information, and an adaptation process for changing conditions.
1Planning Variables and Estimation Methods▼

What Gets Planned (PMBOK §2.4.1–2.4.9)

Scope and Requirements
What will be delivered. WBS decomposes scope into work packages. 100% Rule — everything and nothing more.
Schedule
When will work be done. Critical Path Method for predictive. Sprint plans for agile. Rolling wave for uncertainty.
Cost and Budget
Budget at Completion (BAC). Cost baseline + contingency reserve = project budget. Management reserve separate.
Resources
Who and what is needed. RACI matrix. Resource calendar. Availability planning.
Risk
Risk register. Response plans. Contingency reserve for known unknowns. Management reserve for unknown unknowns.
Quality
Quality management plan. Acceptance criteria. Metrics. QA (process) vs QC (output).
Communications
Who needs what information, when, how, in what format. Channels = N(N-1)/2.
Stakeholder Engagement
Stakeholder Engagement Plan. Tailored communication strategies per stakeholder group.

Estimation Methods

MethodApproachAccuracySpeed
AnalogousUse actuals from similar past projectsLow (±25-75%)Fast
ParametricStatistical relationship: cost-per-unit × volumeMediumFast
Bottom-UpEstimate each work package, aggregate upwardHigh (±5-10%)Slow
Three-Point/PERTE = (O + 4M + P)/6 · σ = (P-O)/6Medium-HighMedium
Planning PokerAgile: simultaneous reveal, discuss discrepancies, consensusRelativeFast
AffinityGroup user stories into T-shirt sizes (S/M/L/XL)Low (relative)Very Fast
📝 Cone of Uncertainty
Early estimates: ±50% accuracy. After requirements: ±30%. After design: ±15%. After build: ±5-10%. Predictive projects progressively narrow the cone. In agile, sprint-level estimates are much more accurate than release-level forecasts.

D5 — Project Work Performance Domain
PMBOK Guide §2.5
📚 PMBOK 7 Guide §2.5
📌 Definition
Addresses activities associated with establishing project processes, managing physical resources, and fostering a learning environment. Effective execution yields: efficient project performance, appropriate processes, effective stakeholder communication, efficient physical resource management, effective procurement, improved team capability through learning.
1Project Processes and Constraints▼

Process Optimisation (PMBOK §2.5.1)

  • Lean production methods: Value stream mapping identifies value-adding vs non-value-adding activities. Eliminate waste (muda).
  • Retrospectives/Lessons Learned: Regular team reviews of HOW they work — suggest process improvements.
  • "Where is the next best dollar spent?" Continuous question to prioritise value delivery over process adherence.
  • Processes must be both efficient (no waste) AND effective (meets quality/regulatory requirements).
  • Process audits and quality assurance ensure processes are followed and accomplishing intended outcomes.

Balancing Competing Constraints (PMBOK §2.5.2)

Constraints: fixed delivery dates, regulatory codes, predetermined budget, quality policies, triple bottom line (economic, social, environmental). Constraints shift throughout the project — balancing is continuous.

Triple Constraint (Iron Triangle)
Scope · Schedule · Cost. Change one = affect the others. Quality is sometimes added as a fourth dimension. PM must communicate trade-offs to sponsor when constraints conflict.
Triple Bottom Line
Economic (financial performance) · Social (impact on people and communities) · Environmental (ecological footprint). PMBOK 7 stewardship principle requires all three to be considered.

Project Team Focus (PMBOK §2.5.3)

  • Balance workload — prevent burnout and disengagement
  • Assess if team members are satisfied with their work (satisfaction correlates with productivity)
  • Maximise overall delivered value = protect production (delivery) AND production capability (team health)

Knowledge Management (PMBOK §2.5.8)

Explicit Knowledge
Can be codified using symbols (words, numbers, pictures). Documents, policies, procedures, plans. Easy to store and transfer.
Tacit Knowledge
Personal knowledge difficult to articulate: beliefs, experience, insights, intuition. Transferred through apprenticeship, storytelling, collaboration.
⭐ EXAM
Lessons Learned Register captures BOTH explicit (documented findings) and tacit (experiential insights) knowledge. It is a project document updated throughout the project and becomes an Organisational Process Asset at closure.

D6 — Delivery Performance Domain
PMBOK Guide §2.6
📚 PMBOK 7 Guide §2.6
📌 Definition
Addresses activities associated with delivering the scope and quality that the project was undertaken to achieve. Effective execution yields: deliverables that contribute to business objectives, project outcomes that realise intended benefits, schedule and budget met, satisfied stakeholders who understand the expected outcomes and value.
1Scope, Quality, Subdeliverables▼

Scope Delivery (PMBOK §2.6.1)

  • Requirements must be collected, analysed, and prioritised to identify what is in and out of scope
  • Requirements Traceability Matrix (RTM) links requirements to deliverables — every requirement must be addressed
  • Validate Scope = formal customer acceptance of deliverables (not the same as QC verification)
  • Agile: User Stories + Acceptance Criteria + Definition of Done serve the same purpose as scope baseline

Quality Delivery (PMBOK §2.6.2)

Quality Assurance (QA)
Proactive. Focus on PROCESSES. During execution. Prevent defects from entering deliverables. Audits, process reviews.
Quality Control (QC)
Reactive. Focus on OUTPUTS. After work is done. Find defects in deliverables. Inspection, testing, control charts.
Cost of Quality (CoQ)
Conformance: Prevention + Appraisal. Non-Conformance: Internal Failure + External Failure. External failure most expensive.
Continuous Improvement
Retrospectives (agile) and lessons learned (predictive) are the mechanisms. Kaizen mindset: every process can improve.

Subdeliverables (PMBOK §2.6.3)

  • In predictive: work packages → activity outputs → phase deliverables → project deliverable
  • In agile: user stories → sprint increment → release → product
  • Definition of Done (DoD) — agile commitment for what "complete" means for each increment
  • Definition of Ready (DoR) — criteria for a user story to enter a sprint
📌 DoD vs Acceptance Criteria
DoD = team-level quality gate applied to every increment (shared across all stories). Acceptance Criteria = story-specific conditions the customer must agree are met for a story to be accepted. DoD applies even if acceptance criteria are met.

D7 — Measurement Performance Domain
PMBOK Guide §2.7
📚 PMBOK 7 Guide §2.7
📌 Definition
Addresses activities associated with assessing project performance and taking appropriate actions to maintain acceptable performance. Effective execution yields: reliable project status understanding, actionable decision-making data, timely corrective actions, accurate forecasts and achieved targets.
1Metrics, EVM, and Measurement Pitfalls▼

Effective Metrics (PMBOK §2.7.1)

Only measure what will allow learning, facilitate decisions, improve performance, help avoid issues, or prevent deterioration. Measure what matters — not what is easy to measure.

Predictive Metrics
EVM (PV, EV, AC, CPI, SPI, EAC, ETC, VAC, TCPI), schedule variance, cost variance, earned value, burndown/burnup, milestone completion rate
Agile Metrics
Velocity (story points/sprint), cycle time, lead time, throughput, WIP limits, cumulative flow, defect density, team satisfaction

EVM — Complete Formula Reference

PV | EV | AC | BAC
PV=%Planned×BAC | EV=%Done×BAC | AC=actual spent
BAC=total approved budget. EV is ALWAYS the numerator in indices.
CV | SV | CPI | SPI
CV=EV−AC | SV=EV−PV | CPI=EV/AC | SPI=EV/PV
>0 or >1 = good. <0 or <1 = bad. EV FIRST in variances.
EAC (CPI, most common)
EAC = BAC ÷ CPI
Use when current performance will continue for remaining work
EAC (atypical) | ETC | VAC
EAC=AC+(BAC−EV) | ETC=EAC−AC | VAC=BAC−EAC
Atypical = one-time variance. ETC = money left. VAC = over/under at end.
TCPI | PERT | Channels
TCPI=(BAC−EV)/(BAC−AC) | E=(O+4M+P)/6 | C=N(N−1)/2
TCPI>1.1=unrealistic. σ=(P−O)/6. Add stakeholder = N new channels.

Measurement Pitfalls (PMBOK §2.7.4 — All 6)

PitfallWhat HappensHow to Avoid
Hawthorne EffectThe act of measuring changes behaviour. Team focuses on the metric, not the goal.Choose metrics that represent true value, not proxies
Vanity MetricShows data but provides no actionable insight. Looks good, means nothing.Only measure what enables decisions or learning
DemoralizationUnreachable goals cause team to stop tryingBalance stretch goals with achievable targets. Recognise effort.
Misusing MetricsFocusing on easy/less important metrics, gaming the systemRegularly review whether metrics still measure what matters
Confirmation BiasSeeking data that confirms existing views, ignoring contradicting dataActively look for disconfirming data; diverse team perspectives
Correlation ≠ CausationTwo variables move together does not mean one causes the otherInvestigate root cause before assuming causal relationship
⭐ EXAM
Thresholds: define acceptable variance ranges upfront. Exception plan: pre-agreed actions when threshold is breached. Do NOT wait for breach — forecast trends and act proactively. PMBOK §2.7.5 example: budget threshold +10%/-20% triggers exception plan.

D8 — Uncertainty Performance Domain
PMBOK Guide §2.8
📚 PMBOK 7 Guide §2.8
📌 Definition
Addresses activities associated with risk and uncertainty. Effective execution yields: awareness of environment, proactive uncertainty exploration, understanding variable interdependencies, capacity to anticipate threats and opportunities, little negative impact from unforeseen events, opportunities realised, reserves used effectively.
1Uncertainty, Risk, Ambiguity, Complexity▼

Key Terms (PMBOK §2.8 Definitions)

TermPMBOK DefinitionKey Distinction
UncertaintyLack of understanding and awareness of issues, events, paths to follow, or solutionsBroadest term — umbrella concept
RiskUncertain event or condition that, if it occurs, has positive or negative effect on objectivesSpecific, identifiable, has probability and impact
AmbiguityState of being unclear, difficulty identifying cause, multiple options from which to choose"We don't know what we don't know" — epistemic
ComplexityCharacteristic difficult to manage due to human behaviour, system behaviour, and ambiguityEmergent, non-linear, unpredictable outcomes
VolatilityPossibility for rapid and unpredictable changeSpeed and unpredictability of environmental change

Risk Responses

Threats (Negative Risks)
Avoid (eliminate) · Transfer (shift to 3rd party) · Mitigate (reduce P or I) · Accept (active: contingency plan; passive: absorb) · Escalate (beyond authority)
Opportunities (Positive Risks)
Exploit (ensure happens) · Share (partner for mutual benefit) · Enhance (increase P or I) · Accept · Escalate (beyond authority)
Contingency Reserve
For KNOWN unknowns (identified risks). IN the cost baseline. PM can approve use within authority. Tracked in EVM calculations.
Management Reserve
For UNKNOWN unknowns. NOT in cost baseline. NOT in EVM. Requires sponsor/senior management approval to release.

Complexity Navigation Approaches

  • Systems thinking: Consider interconnections and cascading effects before acting
  • Decomposition: Break complex problems into smaller, more manageable components
  • Experimentation: Small safe-to-fail experiments to probe complex systems
  • Diverse perspectives: Invite multiple viewpoints to see blind spots
  • Stacey Matrix: Simple (both clear→predictive), Complicated (one clear→experts), Complex (both uncertain→agile), Chaotic (both unknown→act first, then sense)

Tailoring — PMBOK Guide Section 3
PMBOK Guide §3
📚 PMBOK 7 Guide §3 — Tailoring
📌 Core Concept
Tailoring is the deliberate adaptation of approach, governance, and processes to make them more suitable for the project environment. PMBOK 7 makes tailoring CENTRAL — not optional. Every project is unique and deserves a purposefully adapted approach. Tailoring ≠ ignoring processes.
1What Gets Tailored and Why▼

Tailoring Targets

Life Cycle and Approach
Select predictive, iterative, incremental, agile, or hybrid. Match to project characteristics. Most impactful tailoring decision.
Processes
Which processes to use, simplify, combine, or omit. Large complex projects need more process. Small simple projects need less.
Engagement and Communication
Formality level, frequency, channels, formats adapted to stakeholder needs and culture.
Tools and Methods
Project management tools, reporting tools, collaboration platforms. Choose what serves the team best.
Governance
Decision rights, approvals required, escalation paths, reporting requirements. Match organisational context.
Artifacts
Templates, documents, plans. Minimise to what adds value. Large portfolios may need more artifacts; small projects less.

Tailoring Process (PMBOK §3.2 — 4 Steps)

  • Select initial development approach: Consider product/deliverable, team, organisation factors. Predictive, agile, or hybrid starting point.
  • Tailor for the organisation: Apply organisational governance, standards, PMO requirements, and existing processes as a starting framework.
  • Tailor for the project: Adapt further based on specific project characteristics, team composition, stakeholder needs, and constraints.
  • Implement ongoing improvement: Continuously evaluate and adapt through retrospectives, lessons learned, and performance feedback.
💡 KEY DISTINCTION
Tailoring is COLLABORATIVE — done WITH the team and PMO, not unilaterally by the PM. The team owns how they work; the PM facilitates the selection. PMO provides the starting framework; the team adapts it.
2Tailoring by Approach Type▼
FactorTailor Toward PredictiveTailor Toward Agile
Requirements certaintyHigh — requirements fixed and complete upfrontLow — requirements will emerge and evolve
Technology uncertaintyLow — proven, familiar technologyHigh — novel or experimental technology
Stakeholder availabilityLow — stakeholders can only engage at milestonesHigh — daily collaboration possible
Team experienceStrong domain expertise, less agile experienceExperienced with agile methods
Regulatory constraintsHigh — regulated industry requiring documentationLow — flexible environment
Project sizeLarge, long-duration projects benefit from upfront planningSmall, fast-moving projects benefit from iteration
Risk toleranceLow — organisation risk-averse, wants predictabilityHigh — organisation embraces uncertainty and learning
⚠️ COMMON EXAM TRAP
"Transitioning to agile" does NOT mean immediately replacing everything. The correct answer is always: gradual introduction, pilot project first, inception deck, training before full rollout. Mohammad Rehman Principle 15: Go gradually. Abrupt transitions fail.

Models, Methods & Artifacts (MMA)
PMBOK Guide §4
📚 PMBOK 7 Guide §4 — Models, Methods, and Artifacts
📌 Overview
MMA section provides a reference of commonly used project management tools and techniques. PMBOK 7 does not prescribe which to use — that is determined by tailoring. Models explain how something works. Methods define means to achieve outcomes. Artifacts are documents, data, or deliverables.
1Models — Leadership, Team, Change, Complexity▼

Leadership Models

Situational Leadership (Hersey-Blanchard)
D1(Low comp+High commit)→S1 Directing | D2(Low-Med+Low)→S2 Coaching | D3(Med-High+Variable)→S3 Supporting | D4(High+High)→S4 Delegating
OSCAR Coaching Model
Outcome→Situation→Choices→Actions→Review. Structured coaching conversation. PM as coach uses this to help team members find their own solutions.
Servant Leadership
Leader serves the team. Remove obstacles. Enable delivery. Protect from distractions. Develop people. Build trust through consistency. PMP exam default leadership style.
Transformational Leadership
Inspire through vision. Change mindsets and values. Long-term culture change. High charisma. Creates dependency risk if leader leaves.

Team Development Models

Tuckman (Forming-Storming-Norming-Performing-Adjourning)
Non-linear. Teams can regress. New member → team may return to Storming. PM adapts leadership style at each stage.
Drexler/Sibbet Team Performance Model
7 stages: Orientation, Trust Building, Goal Clarification, Commitment, Implementation, High Performance, Renewal. More detailed than Tuckman.

Motivation Models

ModelKey ConceptExam Application
Maslow5-level hierarchy. Lower before higher.Cannot motivate with self-actualisation if safety needs unmet.
HerzbergHygiene prevents dissatisfaction. Motivators create motivation.Salary=hygiene (not motivator). Achievement/growth=motivators.
McGregor X/YX=lazy/control. Y=self-motivated/trust.PMP assumes Theory Y. Agile embodies Theory Y.
McClellandnAch, nAff, nPow.nAch = needs challenging work with feedback.
Vroom ExpectancyMotivation=Expectancy×Instrumentality×Valence.All three must be positive for motivation to exist.
Theory Z (Ouchi)Long-term employment, collective decisions, holistic employee care.Japanese management model. Builds deep loyalty.

Change Models

ADKAR (Prosci)
Awareness→Desire→Knowledge→Ability→Reinforcement. Sequential — cannot skip. Identify the failing stage to target intervention. Most tested on PMP.
Kotter 8-Step
Urgency→Coalition→Vision→Communicate→Enable→Short Wins→Consolidate→Anchor. Organisational transformation model.
Lewin 3-Stage
Unfreeze (create motivation)→Change (transition)→Refreeze (stabilise). Simple, powerful for individual and small group change.
Virginia Satir Change Curve
Old status quo→Foreign element→Chaos→Transforming idea→Practice→New status quo. Performance DIPS before rising. Expect regression.

Complexity Models

Stacey Matrix
x-axis: certainty of technology. y-axis: agreement on requirements. Simple (both clear)→predictive. Complicated (one clear)→expert methods. Complex (both uncertain)→agile. Chaotic→act first.
VUCA
Volatile (fast change), Uncertain (unpredictable), Complex (many interactions), Ambiguous (multiple interpretations). High VUCA = agile. Low VUCA = predictive.
2Methods — Data Gathering, Analysis, Estimation, Meetings▼

Data Gathering & Analysis Methods

Brainstorming
Generate ideas without criticism. All ideas welcome. Follow-up with evaluation and prioritisation. Good for risk identification and requirements gathering.
Benchmarking
Compare practices, processes, or results against best-in-class competitors or other industries. Identifies improvement opportunities and sets performance targets.
Root Cause Analysis
5 Whys technique, Fishbone/Ishikawa diagram (6Ms: Machine, Method, Material, Man, Measurement, Mother Nature). Find the actual cause, not the symptom.
Decision Tree / EMV
Diagrams choices and outcomes. EMV = Probability × Impact. Sum all branch EMVs to select optimal path. Used in quantitative risk analysis.
Monte Carlo Simulation
Runs thousands of scenarios with varying inputs. Outputs S-curve of probability distributions for cost and schedule. Reveals likelihood of achieving targets.
SWOT Analysis
Strengths, Weaknesses (internal), Opportunities, Threats (external). Used for risk identification and strategic planning.

3Artifacts — Documents, Plans, Baselines, Registers▼

Key Logs and Registers

Assumption Log
Records all assumptions made during planning. Assumptions that prove false become risks or issues. Updated throughout project.
Risk Register
All identified risks: description, probability, impact, priority, response strategy, owner, status, triggers. Living document — updated continuously.
Issue Log
Records current problems requiring resolution. Risks that have materialised become issues. Tracks owner, priority, resolution date.
Change Log
Records all change requests, their status (submitted/reviewed/approved/rejected/deferred), and impact assessments.
Lessons Learned Register
Captures positive and negative experiences throughout project. Becomes OPA at closure. Requires psychological safety for honest capture.
Stakeholder Register
Name, role, interest, influence, current/desired engagement level, contact info, strategy. Confidential. Updated as stakeholders change.

Three Baselines (PMB)

  • Scope Baseline: Approved scope statement + WBS + WBS Dictionary. Defines what will be delivered.
  • Schedule Baseline: Approved project schedule. Defines when activities will occur.
  • Cost Baseline: Approved time-phased project budget (WITHOUT management reserve). Used for EVM.
  • Together: Performance Measurement Baseline (PMB). Changes require formal CCB approval.

Visual Data Displays

Burndown Chart
Y=remaining work, X=time. Shows if sprint/release is on track. Ideal line for reference. Falls toward zero as work completes.
Burnup Chart
Shows work completed over time. Scope changes visible as ceiling rises. Better for communicating scope creep impact.
Cumulative Flow Diagram
Shows work volume in each workflow state over time. Parallel bands = healthy flow. Widening band = bottleneck forming.
S-Curve
Cumulative cost or work over time. Slow start, rapid middle, tapering end. Used to baseline planned vs actual cost/schedule.

P18 — Never Immediately Reject a Request — Especially from a Client

Glossary of Key PMP Terms
Exam Reference
📚 PMBOK 7 Glossary · PMI Agile Practice Guide · PMP Exam Content Outline
💡 How to Use
These are the terms most likely to appear in exam questions. Know the PMBOK definition — not just the everyday meaning. Several common traps come from misreading a term.
AA–F Key Terms▼
Acceptance Criteria — Conditions that must be satisfied for a deliverable to be accepted by stakeholders. Story-specific. Different from DoD.
Accountability — Being answerable for an outcome. CANNOT be shared or delegated. Rests with ONE person.
Activity — Distinct, scheduled portion of work performed during a project. Lowest level of schedule network.
ADKAR — Awareness, Desire, Knowledge, Ability, Reinforcement. Sequential change model. Find the failing stage to target intervention.
Agile — Iterative and incremental approach. Frequent delivery, rapid feedback, self-organising teams. Responds to change over following a plan.
Ambiguity — State of being unclear; difficulty in identifying cause of events or multiple interpretable options. Broader than risk.
Analogous Estimating — Using actuals from similar past projects. Fast, less accurate. Best for early-phase estimates.
Assumption — Factor considered true without proof. Assumptions that prove false become risks or issues. Recorded in Assumption Log.
Authority — Right to apply resources, make decisions, sign approvals. Can be delegated. Formally granted.
Backlog — Ordered list of work to be done (Product Backlog) or sprint commitments (Sprint Backlog). Owned by Product Owner and Developers respectively.
BAC — Budget at Completion. Total approved project budget. Fixed unless formally changed via CCB.
Baseline — Approved plan (scope, schedule, or cost) against which performance is measured. Changes require CCB approval.
BCR — Benefit-Cost Ratio. Benefits ÷ Costs. >1 = benefits exceed costs. Higher = better project selection.
Bottom-Up Estimating — Estimate each work package, aggregate upward. Most accurate. Most time-consuming.
Burndown Chart — Shows remaining work (Y) over time (X). Falls toward zero as work completes. Used in agile sprint tracking.
Burnup Chart — Shows work completed over time. Scope changes visible as ceiling rises. Better for communicating scope change impact.
Business Case — Economic feasibility study and justification for project. Contains: business need, NPV, ROI, strategic alignment, risk. Maintained by sponsor.
CCB — Change Control Board. Reviews, evaluates, approves, or rejects change requests. Even sponsor cannot bypass CCB.
Change Log — Documents all change requests, their status, and impact assessments. Maintained throughout project.
Change Request — Formal proposal to modify any document, deliverable, or baseline. Must go through CCB in predictive projects.
CoQ — Cost of Quality. Conformance (Prevention + Appraisal) + Non-Conformance (Internal + External Failure). Prevention is cheapest.
Complexity — Characteristic difficult to manage due to human behaviour, system behaviour, and ambiguity. Non-linear, emergent outcomes.
Cone of Uncertainty — Estimates improve in accuracy as project progresses. Early: ±50%. Final: ±5-10%.
Contingency Reserve — Budget held for known risks (known unknowns). IN cost baseline. PM can approve within authority level.
CPI — Cost Performance Index. EV÷AC. >1 = under budget. <1 = over budget. Best predictor of final cost.
Critical Path — Longest duration network path. Determines minimum project duration. TF=0 on all critical path activities.
CV — Cost Variance. EV−AC. Positive = under budget. Negative = over budget.
Cycle Time — Time from work starting to work completing. Key Kanban metric.
Daily Scrum — 15-minute event. Developers inspect Sprint Goal progress. NOT a status meeting for PM or management.
Definition of Done (DoD) — Team-level quality criteria for an Increment to be complete. Created by Developers. Cannot be weakened mid-Sprint.
Definition of Ready (DoR) — Criteria a user story must meet before entering a sprint. Story written, estimates done, dependencies resolved.
Deliverable — Any unique, verifiable product, result, or capability to perform a service that is required to be produced to complete a process.
Development Approach — The means used to create and evolve a product: predictive, iterative, incremental, agile, or hybrid.
EAC — Estimate at Completion. Forecast of total project cost. Most common formula: BAC÷CPI.
EMV — Expected Monetary Value. Probability × Impact. Used in decision trees and quantitative risk analysis.
ETC — Estimate to Complete. EAC−AC. How much MORE money is needed to finish from today.
EV — Earned Value. % Work Complete × BAC. Value of work actually performed. Always in numerator of EVM indices.
EVM — Earned Value Management. Integrates scope, schedule, and cost. Uses PV, EV, AC to measure performance and forecast outcomes.
Fast-Tracking — Schedule compression by doing activities in parallel. No cost increase. Increases rework risk. Critical path only.
FFP — Firm Fixed Price contract. Seller bears all cost overrun risk. Lowest risk for buyer. Best for defined scope.
Float/Slack — Amount of time an activity can be delayed without delaying project end (Total Float) or successor (Free Float).
GG–Z Key Terms▼
Gold Plating — Adding features beyond what was agreed without change request. Initiated by PM/team. Always a bad practice.
Governance — Framework of authority, roles, decision-making processes for directing and controlling projects and organisations.
Hybrid — Development approach combining predictive and agile elements. Most common in practice.
Inception Deck — Agile tool for aligning team and stakeholders on project vision, approach, and priorities at project start.
Increment — Scrum artifact. Usable, done, potentially shippable output from a sprint. Commitment = Definition of Done.
IRR — Internal Rate of Return. Discount rate where NPV=0. Higher = more attractive. Compare to hurdle rate.
Issue — A risk that has materialised. A current problem requiring resolution. Tracked in Issue Log.
Issue Log — Document tracking current problems: description, owner, priority, resolution date.
Kanban — Visual flow-based method. WIP limits, pull system, cycle time metric. No fixed roles or time-boxes.
KPI — Key Performance Indicator. Metric tied to a specific project or business objective. Must be actionable and relevant.
Lead Time — Time from customer request to delivery. Includes wait time in queue + Cycle Time.
Leads and Lags — Lead=advance successor before predecessor finishes (negative lag). Lag=mandatory wait time between activities.
Lessons Learned Register — Captures what worked and what did not. Created throughout project. Becomes OPA at closure.
Management Reserve — Budget for unknown unknowns. NOT in cost baseline. NOT in EVM. Requires sponsor approval.
Milestone — Significant point or event in a project. Zero duration. Used to mark phase completions and key deliverables.
Monte Carlo Simulation — Runs thousands of scenarios with varying inputs. Outputs probability distribution for cost/schedule outcomes.
MVP — Minimum Viable Product. Smallest deliverable that provides value and enables learning. Core agile/lean concept.
NPV — Net Present Value. Sum of discounted future cash flows minus initial investment. Higher = better. >0 = accept project.
OPA — Organisational Process Assets. Templates, processes, lessons learned, historical data from the organisation. Used and updated by projects.
Opportunity — A risk with a positive effect on project objectives. Responses: Exploit, Share, Enhance, Accept, Escalate.
Outcome — End result or consequence of a process or project. Different from Output — outcomes are changes in behaviour or capability.
Parametric Estimating — Statistical relationship between variables (e.g. cost per unit × units). More accurate than analogous.
Payback Period — Time to recover initial investment. Shorter = better. Does NOT account for time value of money.
PERT — Program Evaluation and Review Technique. E=(O+4M+P)/6. σ=(P−O)/6. Three-point estimate with weighted average.
PMB — Performance Measurement Baseline. Scope + Schedule + Cost baselines combined. Used for EVM. Excludes management reserve.
PMO — Project Management Office. Supportive (guidance), Controlling (compliance), or Directive (manages projects).
Portfolio — Collection of projects, programs, operations aligned to strategic objectives. No fixed end date.
Product Backlog — Scrum artifact. Ordered list of everything needed in product. Owned by Product Owner. Commitment = Product Goal.
Product Owner — Scrum role. Owns Product Backlog. Maximises value. Only person who can cancel a Sprint. Accepts/rejects increment.
Program — Group of related projects managed in a coordinated way to obtain benefits not available from managing individually.
Project Charter — Document formally authorising project. Names PM and grants authority. Issued by Sponsor. Created in Initiating.
Psychological Safety — Belief that one can speak up, admit mistakes, and challenge ideas without fear. #1 predictor of team effectiveness.
PV — Planned Value. % Work Planned × BAC. Authorised budget for scheduled work.
Quality — Degree to which deliverable meets requirements and expectations. "Fitness for purpose" and conformance to requirements.
RACI — Responsibility Assignment Matrix. Responsible, Accountable, Consulted, Informed. Only ONE person can be Accountable per row.
Requirements Traceability Matrix (RTM) — Links requirements to their source and tracks to deliverables. Ensures all requirements are addressed.
Residual Risk — Risk remaining AFTER a response is implemented. Must be accepted and monitored.
Retrospective — Scrum event. Team inspects process, tools, and interactions. Identifies improvements. 3hr max (1-month sprint).
Risk — Uncertain event or condition that, if occurs, has positive or negative effect on objectives. Risk ≠ Issue.
Risk Register — Repository of all identified risks with probability, impact, response, owner, and status. Living document.
Rolling Wave Planning — Near-term work planned in detail; far-future work at high level. Updated as project progresses.
Scope Creep — Uncontrolled expansion of scope without formal change control. Always bad. Prevented by change management.
Scrum Master — Servant leader. Facilitates Scrum events. Removes impediments. Does NOT manage or assign tasks to team.
Secondary Risk — New risk CREATED BY implementing a risk response. Must be identified and managed.
SME — Subject Matter Expert. Consulted when team lacks expertise. External resource brought in for specialised knowledge.
SPI — Schedule Performance Index. EV÷PV. >1 = ahead of schedule. <1 = behind schedule.
Sprint — Scrum time-box of 1–4 weeks. Contains all work and events. No changes that endanger Sprint Goal.
Sprint Backlog — Scrum artifact. Selected PB items + delivery plan. Owned by Developers. Commitment = Sprint Goal.
Stakeholder — Individual, group, or organisation that may affect, be affected by, or perceive itself affected by a project decision or outcome.
Stakeholder Register — Document containing information about identified stakeholders. Confidential. Updated throughout project.
SV — Schedule Variance. EV−PV. Positive = ahead of schedule. Negative = behind schedule.
Systems Thinking — Holistic view of how parts interact. Changes in one area cascade to others. Core PMBOK 7 principle.
T&M Contract — Time and Material. Buyer pays for actual time and materials. No cost ceiling. Highest buyer risk after cost-plus.
Tailoring — Deliberate adaptation of approach, governance, processes to fit the specific project context. Collaborative decision.
TCPI — To Complete Performance Index. (BAC−EV)÷(BAC−AC). Efficiency needed to meet original budget. >1.1 = unrealistic.
Threat — A risk with negative effect on objectives. Responses: Avoid, Transfer, Mitigate, Accept, Escalate.
Throughput — Number of items completed per time period. Used for Kanban forecasting.
Total Float — TF = LS−ES = LF−EF. Time activity can delay without delaying project end. CP activities have TF=0.
Tuckman Model — Forming → Storming → Norming → Performing → Adjourning. Non-linear. Teams can regress.
VAC — Variance at Completion. BAC−EAC. Positive = will finish under budget. Negative = will finish over budget.
Value — Ultimate indicator of project success. Net worth or importance of benefits. Outputs→Outcomes→Benefits→Value.
Velocity — Story points completed per sprint. Used for release forecasting. NEVER compare velocity across different teams.
VUCA — Volatile, Uncertain, Complex, Ambiguous. High VUCA = agile preferred. Low VUCA = predictive works well.
WBS — Work Breakdown Structure. Hierarchical decomposition of total project scope. 100% Rule. Lowest level = Work Package.
WIP Limits — Maximum items in any Kanban workflow state. Exposes bottlenecks. Core Kanban practice.
XP — Extreme Programming. Agile framework: TDD, pair programming, CI, refactoring, collective code ownership.

EVM — Master Formula Reference
PV · EV · AC · BAC
PV=%Planned×BAC | EV=%Done×BAC | AC=actual spent
BAC=total budget. EV ALWAYS numerator in indices. EV ALWAYS first in variances.
CV | SV | CPI | SPI
CV=EV−AC | SV=EV−PV | CPI=EV/AC | SPI=EV/PV
>0 or >1 = good. <0 or <1 = bad.
EAC (CPI, most common)
EAC = BAC ÷ CPI
Current performance will continue. Most accurate predictor.
EAC (atypical) | EAC (CPI+SPI)
EAC=AC+(BAC−EV) | EAC=AC+[(BAC−EV)÷(CPI×SPI)]
Atypical=one-time variance. CPI+SPI=schedule affects cost.
ETC | VAC | TCPI
ETC=EAC−AC | VAC=BAC−EAC | TCPI=(BAC−EV)÷(BAC−AC)
ETC=money left. VAC=over/under at end. TCPI>1.1=unrealistic.
PERT | σ | Channels | Float | EMV
E=(O+4M+P)/6 | σ=(P−O)/6 | C=N(N−1)/2 | TF=LS−ES | EMV=P×I
1σ=68%, 2σ=95%, 3σ=99.7%. CP: TF=0.
EXAMPLE: BAC=00k, PV=0k, EV=0k, AC=5k
CV=−5k OVER | SV=−10k BEHIND | CPI=0.91 | SPI=0.83 | EAC=09.9k | VAC=−.9k
Both under 1: over budget AND behind schedule simultaneously.

Common Exam Traps — Don't Fall For These
  • Trap 1: "Give a raise to motivate" → Salary is HYGIENE not a motivator. Answer: recognition, growth, achievement.
  • Trap 2: "Agile means no documentation" → FALSE. Right side of manifesto still has value. Just less.
  • Trap 3: Sponsor can approve changes without CCB → FALSE. CCB is mandatory. Even sponsor cannot bypass.
  • Trap 4: Management Reserve is in the cost baseline → FALSE. NOT in baseline, NOT in EVM.
  • Trap 5: Accountability can be shared → FALSE. Only ONE person is accountable for any outcome.
  • Trap 6: Gold plating adds value → FALSE. Always bad. Introduces risk and wastes resources.
  • Trap 7: PM should escalate issues to sponsor → FALSE. PM handles issues. Sponsor = budget only, last resort.
  • Trap 8: CPI and SPI are equally important predictors → FALSE. CPI is the BETTER predictor of final cost.
  • Trap 9: TCPI>1.0 = achievable target → FALSE. TCPI>1.1 = generally unrealistic. Challenge the plan.
  • Trap 10: Scope Creep and Gold Plating are the same → FALSE. Scope creep = stakeholder-driven (uncontrolled). Gold plating = PM/team-initiated.
  • Trap 11: SV=0 at project end always → TRUE. Because EV always equals PV at project completion.
  • Trap 12: Payback period accounts for time value of money → FALSE. NPV and IRR account for it. Payback does NOT.
  • Trap 13: Scrum Master assigns tasks to Developers → FALSE. Developers self-organise and pull work themselves.
  • Trap 14: Free Float and Total Float are the same → FALSE. FF = delay without affecting successor. TF = delay without affecting project end.
Key Distinctions — Quick Reference
  • Risk vs Issue: Risk=future, uncertain. Issue=present, definite (materialised risk).
  • QA vs QC: QA=process/proactive. QC=output/reactive.
  • Output vs Outcome: Output=deliverable. Outcome=change enabled by deliverable.
  • DoD vs Acceptance Criteria: DoD=team-wide standard. AC=story-specific conditions.
  • Validate Scope vs Verify Quality: Validate=customer accepts deliverable. Verify=QC confirms it meets specs.
  • Explicit vs Tacit Knowledge: Explicit=can be documented. Tacit=personal experience/intuition.
  • Crashing vs Fast-Tracking: Crash=add resources (costs more). Fast-track=overlap activities (more risk).
